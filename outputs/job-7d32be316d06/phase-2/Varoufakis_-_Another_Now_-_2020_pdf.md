# Concept Appropriation Tracker: *Another Now* (2020) as Prior Work

## Orientation to the Task

This pass traces migration and acknowledgment — how concepts moved from *Another Now* into the Technofeudalism corpus, what mechanisms carried them, and what the pattern of citation and silence reveals about the politics of self-relation. The prior concept_evolution analysis has already mapped *what* changed between 2020 and 2023. This pass asks *how* it traveled, *whether* it was acknowledged, and *why* specific choices were made about what to name and what to leave silent.

The central discovery that frames everything: the prescriptions precede the diagnosis by three years. *Another Now* (2020) contains the complete institutional blueprint (PerCap, Kosmos, PASS, corpo-syndicalism, Citizens' Juries, cloud mobilization logic) before Varoufakis developed the technofeudal analytical framework that is supposed to justify these prescriptions. This reversal is the master fact that every migration path and every acknowledgment decision must be read against.

---

## I. Migration Paths

### Path 1: The Institutional Prescriptions — Explicit, Wholesale Transportation

The most extensively traveled migration path is also the most visible. The institutional architecture of the Other Now — PerCap (Personal Capital accounts), Legacy, Dividend, Accumulation, Kosmos, PASS, one-employee-one-share-one-vote corporations, Citizens' Juries, Socialworthiness Index — travels from *Another Now* to *Technofeudalism* Chapter 7 through direct citation. Varoufakis explicitly tells readers of *Technofeudalism* to consult *Another Now* for "the detailed blueprint." The migration mechanism is acknowledged self-citation.

But acknowledgment covers a multitude of sins, and the structural reality of this migration is more interesting than the citation would suggest. These institutions migrate *intact* — they are not adapted to the new diagnostic context. The prescriptions were calibrated for the problem the novel describes (defeating the Technostructure / corporate oligarchy) and they arrive in *Technofeudalism* calibrated for a different problem (cloud capital's replacement of markets with fiefs). The same institutional design is being used to address different problems without any modification or explanation. This is not adaptation but transportation — and the distinction matters enormously.

The context of origin: in *Another Now*, PerCap is the institutional mechanism through which the central bank provides everyone direct access to the monetary system, eliminating commercial banks, enabling universal basic income, and supporting the one-employee-one-share corporate structure. It is designed in response to the 2008 crisis and the OC rebellion's insight that capitalist finance could be democratized. The Technostructure, as named enemy in the novel, is the target.

The context of arrival: in *Technofeudalism*, PerCap (as digital wallets, universal basic dividend) is the alternative to cloud capital's domination. It is presented as the mechanism through which the state could fund a post-cloud-capital economy. But cloud capital's specific pathology — behavioral modification through PMBM machines, cloud serfs producing unpaid labor that reproduces cloud fiefs — is not addressed by PerCap. PerCap addresses income inequality and financial exclusion; it does not address the behavioral modification mechanism that Varoufakis has identified as cloud capital's defining feature.

This is the migration path's central analytical finding: the concept traveled but its functional calibration did not change. The prescriptions arrived in a new diagnostic context without being re-aimed at the new target. The acknowledgment ("detailed blueprint in *Another Now*") serves to establish continuity and authority while concealing this mismatch.

### Path 2: The Technostructure — A Silent Concept Transformation

This is the most analytically revealing migration path because it is the one that involves the greatest semantic mutation and receives the least acknowledgment.

In *Another Now* (page 79), the Galbraithian Technostructure is the **contemporary enemy** the OC rebels are fighting. Kosti explicitly identifies the rebel network's goal as bringing down "capitalism's network of power, its agglomeration of mega-firms and mega-banks" — Galbraith's Technostructure. The prescriptions (corpo-syndicalism, PerCap, etc.) are calibrated to defeat this enemy.

In *Technofeudalism* (2023), the same Technostructure has been repositioned as a **historical precursor** that cloud capital already superseded. The Technostructure was the enemy until cloud capital automated its functions. The prescriptions designed to defeat the Technostructure are now being proposed as the solution to a post-Technostructure problem.

The migration mechanism: **silent re-contextualization without acknowledgment**. Varoufakis does not note that in *Another Now*, the Technostructure was the contemporary enemy; he does not explain why it is now a historical antecedent; he does not acknowledge that the prescriptions were designed for a different adversary. The concept's semantic position migrates (from enemy to antecedent) without any marking of the journey.

This migration path reveals something important: between 2020 and 2023, Varoufakis made a genuine conceptual advance — recognizing that Big Tech represented something qualitatively beyond the Technostructure rather than its continuation. But the prescriptions were not updated to reflect this advance. The old solutions were kept; only the diagnosis changed. The silence around this migration serves to conceal the mismatch between diagnosis and prescription.

### Path 3: "Techno-Feudalism" to "Technofeudalism" — Phrase to System

Page 145 of *Another Now* contains: "its returns were only made possible by a species of techno-feudalism that made billions of people work for it for free." This is the proto-coinage — Eva's interior monologue uses it as a descriptive phrase, not yet as a theoretical category.

The migration from descriptive phrase (2020) to master-concept with full analytical apparatus (2023) is a migration of the concept's *epistemic status* rather than its content. The phrase traveled three years and became the organizing term of an entire system of analysis. The mechanism of travel: the phrase existed in Varoufakis's working vocabulary in 2020, crystallized through subsequent talks and papers (as *Technofeudalism*'s Preface notes: "by 2018 the term had already appeared in papers and talks"), and received full theoretical elaboration in the 2023 book.

The acknowledgment status is interesting: *Technofeudalism* never acknowledges that the coinage appears first in *Another Now*. The prior work is cited for the institutional blueprint but not for the terminological origin of the master concept. This is a small instance of a pattern: Varoufakis cites *Another Now* for what he wants to preserve (the prescriptions) but not for what might complicate the narrative (the term's prior appearance as description rather than analysis).

### Path 4: The Cloud Mobilization Logic — Concept Without Name

The "Days of Inaction" logic in *Another Now* — minimal personal sacrifice, maximum collective gain, consumer boycott coordinated with worker strike — is the conceptual precursor to "cloud mobilization" in *Technofeudalism*. The mechanism was described in narrative detail in 2020; it received its formal name in 2023.

The migration preserves the functional logic while replacing the specific instantiation (the Bladerunners targeting Amazon) with a general principle (cloud mobilization as a strategic concept). What travels is the *mechanism*; what does not travel is the specific characters and campaigns that the novel used to dramatize it.

The acknowledgment: *Technofeudalism* does not acknowledge that the cloud mobilization concept was first dramatized in *Another Now*. The citation to *Another Now* in Chapter 7 is for the institutional blueprint, not for the political strategy. This conceals the concept's genealogy — the reader of *Technofeudalism* encounters "cloud mobilization" as a new strategic innovation rather than as a formalization of a narrative concept from the 2020 novel.

### Path 5: The Iris Voice — The Non-Migrating Concept

This is the migration that did not happen, and it may be the most analytically significant path to trace.

In *Another Now*, Iris articulates the most radical critique of the entire prescriptive program: the Other Now's market socialism stabilizes rather than transcends market exchange; it is not enough. Her final position — "I prefer to stay in Our horrid Now than live in a much better version of it that only makes the prospect of a Star Trek communism feel further away" — is the position that market socialism, however democratized, forecloses more radical transformation toward what she calls "the sovereignty of good": unconditional collaboration, gift economy, freedom *from* markets rather than *freer* markets.

This critique does not migrate to *Technofeudalism*. The 2023 book presents the Another Now prescriptions as the alternative to technofeudalism without engaging the Iris-level objection. There is no voice in *Technofeudalism* saying: these prescriptions stabilize market exchange rather than transcending it, and this stabilization is itself a problem.

The mechanism of non-migration: **suppression through format change**. The novel's three-character Socratic dialogue could contain Iris's voice because the format required dramatic conflict. The non-fiction essay format of *Technofeudalism* has no structural slot for a character who ultimately rejects the prescriptions. Iris's voice is incompatible with the book's rhetorical posture of affirmative alternative. The format determines what can be said.

This non-migration has consequences: the most radical internal critique of the prescriptive program was available to Varoufakis in 2020 and was not inherited by the 2023 book. Whether this represents intellectual progress (the arguments were settled) or strategic suppression (the objections were avoided to maintain the affirmative register) is genuinely unclear. But the Iris-voice's absence from *Technofeudalism* means that the most challenging question the prescriptions face — whether they are sufficient or whether they merely perpetuate market logic in a new form — is not asked in the book that presents them most ambitiously.

### Path 6: The Wormhole Frame — Irreplaceable Device, No Migration

The wormhole fiction, the Other Now as discovered alternative reality, HALPEVAM, CREST, the science-fiction apparatus — none of this migrates to *Technofeudalism*. This is the most extensive cluster of non-migration in the entire relationship.

The mechanism of non-migration: **category incompatibility**. The wormhole was doing rhetorical work that is impossible in analytical prose. It converted speculative proposals into documented alternatives — the prescriptions were not *proposed* but *reported from the Other Now*, giving them the epistemic force of discovered fact. This fictional laundering of speculative proposals into apparent empirical reports is irreproducible in the format of a political economy book. What travels instead is the bibliographic citation: "I argued for this in *Another Now*." This is epistemically more honest (the proposals are acknowledged as invented) but rhetorically far weaker.

## II. Acknowledgment Status

### What Is Acknowledged

The citation of *Another Now* in *Technofeudalism* Chapter 7 is explicit and direct. Varoufakis tells readers that for the "detailed blueprint" of the alternative society, they should consult the 2020 novel. This is a genuine acknowledgment of the prescriptive debt. It is also, as will become clear, a selective acknowledgment that acknowledges the most convenient elements while concealing the more problematic ones.

Explicitly acknowledged:
- The Another Now institutional design (PerCap, Kosmos, PASS, democratized corporations, Citizens' Juries)
- The novel as the source of the alternative vision
- The fictional frame of the Other Now as the context in which these proposals were developed

Not acknowledged:
- That the Technostructure was the contemporary enemy in *Another Now* and is a historical antecedent in *Technofeudalism* (the semantic migration of this concept)
- That the Iris-voice critique of market socialism as insufficient exists in the prior work and is not engaged in the current work
- That "techno-feudalism" as a phrase appears in *Another Now* before it becomes the master concept of the 2023 book
- That the cloud mobilization logic was first dramatized in the Bladerunners narrative of the novel
- That the prescriptions were calibrated for a different diagnostic target (the Technostructure) than the one the 2023 book diagnoses (cloud capital)

### The Logic of the Acknowledgment Pattern

What is acknowledged? The institutional prescriptions — the most politically positive content. What is suppressed? The dialectical tensions, the concept migrations, the calibration mismatches, the radical critique.

The pattern serves two rhetorical goals simultaneously:
1. **Establishing continuity and authority**: citing the prior work demonstrates that the prescriptions are not ad hoc responses to the technofeudal diagnosis but part of a sustained intellectual program
2. **Suppressing complications**: not acknowledging the Technostructure's semantic migration, the Iris-voice, or the calibration mismatch allows the argument to present the prescriptions as more adequate to the technofeudal diagnosis than they actually are

The acknowledgment is technically honest (the citation exists) but strategically selective (what is cited creates a specific impression that the full picture would not support). This is the politics of self-citation at its most characteristic: using acknowledgment to establish what one wants established while using silence to suppress what would complicate the argument.

### The Democratic Deficit Framing — Presence Without Development

The "democratic deficit" analysis exists in *Another Now* through its institutional design — all institutions are designed to extend democracy into economic life that capitalism keeps under oligarchic control. But the phrase "democratic deficit" is not a prominent coinage in the novel; it is more visible in the Minotaur works.

What travels from *Another Now* to *Technofeudalism* on this theme: the analysis that cloud capital makes democratic governance structurally more difficult (the behavioral modification apparatus shapes what citizens want and how they relate to each other) represents a *deepening* of the democratic deficit framing. The novel's democratic deficit is remediated by institutional design; *Technofeudalism*'s democratic deficit is structural and possibly irremediable through the same institutional designs. This is not acknowledged — the institutional designs are presented in *Technofeudalism* as the alternative without noting that the novel's own analysis suggests they might not be adequate to the technofeudal form of democratic deficit.

### The "Assertion of Continuity Where Change Occurred"

The prior analysis context identified this as a specific concern. The most significant instance: *Technofeudalism* asserts continuity with *Another Now* through the explicit citation, but the actual relationship involves:
- A diagnostic framework that changed (Technostructure as enemy → Technostructure as historical antecedent)
- A prescriptive framework that stayed the same (the same institutional designs)
- A suppression of the most radical internal critique of those designs (Iris's voice)

The assertion of continuity ("see *Another Now* for the detailed blueprint") is technically accurate about the prescriptive content but misleading about the diagnostic context. The 2023 book is not simply applying the *Another Now* blueprint to a new diagnosis; it is applying that blueprint to a diagnosis it was not designed for, while suppressing the critique that would notice the mismatch.

## III. What the Pattern Reveals

The migration paths and acknowledgment pattern together reveal the following about the politics of self-relation in Varoufakis's intellectual practice:

**First**: The prescriptions were complete before the diagnosis. This is the most structurally significant fact. The diagnostic framework (technofeudalism, cloud capital, PMBM) was developed after the prescriptive program was already worked out. The prescriptions did not flow from the diagnosis — the diagnosis was developed to provide a theoretical justification for prescriptions that already existed. This reversal is concealed by the acknowledgment pattern: citing *Another Now* as the source of the "detailed blueprint" makes the prescriptions seem like derived responses to the technofeudal diagnosis rather than prior inventions that the diagnosis was constructed to justify.

**Second**: The most challenging internal critique of the prescriptions was suppressed when they were transported. Iris's argument — that market socialism perpetuates the logic of conditional exchange and forecloses more radical transformation — is the strongest objection to the institutional design program. Its suppression in *Technofeudalism* serves the book's affirmative political register but leaves the prescriptions' most fundamental vulnerability unaddressed.

**Third**: The silent position revision is most visible in the Technostructure's semantic migration. In 2020, the Technostructure was the contemporary enemy; in 2023, it is historical background. This represents a genuine conceptual advance — the recognition that Big Tech represents something qualitatively beyond the Technostructure. But the prescriptions were not updated to reflect this advance. The institutional designs remain calibrated for defeating the Technostructure through collective action and democratic reform, while the diagnosis has advanced to arguing that cloud capital is structurally immune to such approaches. The advance in diagnosis has not been matched by an advance in prescription, and this mismatch is concealed by the continuity-asserting self-citation.

**Fourth**: The wormhole's irreplaceable rhetorical work was abandoned when the fictional apparatus did not migrate. The novel gave the prescriptions epistemic force by presenting them as discovered rather than invented. The non-fiction book replaces this with a bibliographic citation, which is epistemically more honest but rhetorical weaker. The loss of the wormhole's discovery-frame is the largest single rhetorical cost of the transition from fiction to analysis — and it is not acknowledged because acknowledging it would require admitting that the prescriptions' strongest claim to viability was fictional.

**Fifth and most profound**: The diagnostic-prescriptive reversal revealed by tracing these migration paths is the deepest structural feature of the argument's genealogy, and it is invisible without knowing the prior work. A reader who has not read *Another Now* cannot see that the prescriptions predate the diagnosis; they will naturally assume the diagnosis generated the prescriptions. The acknowledgment pattern — citing the novel for the blueprint — actually *supports* this misreading by presenting the novel as a source that *Technofeudalism* is building on, rather than as a source that *Technofeudalism* is retroactively justifying. The self-citation performs intellectual genealogy while concealing intellectual sequence.

## I. Migration Paths: Following the Logic

### The Institutional Architecture Migration — What the Logic Commits To

The explicit migration of PerCap, Kosmos, PASS, corpo-syndicalism, Citizens' Juries from *Another Now* to *Technofeudalism* Chapter 7 is acknowledged and well-mapped. But the inferential analysis must pursue what follows from this migration.

**If the prescriptions were calibrated for defeating the Technostructure (as the enemy in *Another Now* p.79), and if they are now being used against cloud capital (as the enemy in *Technofeudalism*), then the argument is committed to the claim that the same institutional designs address both problems.**

Following this chain: the Technostructure in *Another Now* is Galbraith's "agglomeration of mega-firms and mega-banks" — the coordinated corporate planning apparatus. Cloud capital in *Technofeudalism* is specifically characterized as the *replacement* of the Technostructure's human professionals with algorithms. If these are sequentially different enemies with different mechanisms, then prescriptions calibrated for one should require modification for the other. The argument never makes this modification explicit.

What the argument is committed to without saying so: either (a) the institutional designs work against both the Technostructure and cloud capital for the same underlying reason (perhaps because both are forms of concentrated economic power resisting democratic accountability), in which case the prescriptions operate at a more abstract level than the specific mechanism of exploitation; or (b) the prescriptions were imported without recalibration, which is an intellectual oversight the argument has not acknowledged. The inferential pressure is toward (b), and the argument avoids the inference by never making (a) explicit.

**The logical chain continues**: If the prescriptions were designed for a different enemy than the one now diagnosed, then their adequacy to the technofeudal condition is assumed rather than demonstrated. Specifically: how does one-employee-one-share-one-vote governance address the behavioral modification mechanism (PMBM)? How does PerCap's central bank digital wallet address the cloud fief structure that replaces markets? These questions are logically required by the migration but are never posed in *Technofeudalism*.

### The Technostructure's Semantic Migration — Inferential Consequences

On *Another Now* p.79, Kosti uses "techno-syndicalism" by adapting Galbraith's term for the OC rebels' network — the Technostructure is the system the rebels are fighting *against* in the present tense. In *Technofeudalism*, the Technostructure is historical background, already superseded by cloud capital.

**What follows from this semantic migration**: If the Technostructure was the enemy in 2020 (when Varoufakis was writing *Another Now*) but by 2023 had been reframed as historical antecedent, then the conceptual advance between the two texts is precisely the recognition that Big Tech had *already moved beyond* the Technostructure. Varoufakis was still fighting the Technostructure (in novelistic imagination) when cloud capital had already superseded it.

The inferential commitment this creates: the prescriptions in *Another Now* were calibrated for defeating a system that, by the time *Technofeudalism* was written, Varoufakis recognized as already historically superseded. The OC rebels' victory in the Other Now solved a problem that, in Our Now, has been replaced by a harder problem. The prescriptions that worked in the novel's counter-factual (where the Technostructure was the terminal enemy) may be insufficient for the actual condition (where cloud capital has superseded the Technostructure).

This is not a small point. The 2026 article's formal model shows that cloud capital is immune to the mechanisms that constrained the Technostructure (price regulation, antitrust, democratic pressure on publicly accountable corporations). If the prescriptions — democratized firms, PerCap, Kosmos — were designed to defeat the Technostructure, they may be no more adequate to cloud capital than the social democratic toolkit they were designed to surpass.

**The logic leads here**: *Another Now*'s OC rebellion succeeded because it could target the Technostructure through collective action and institutional reform. The success was possible because the Technostructure, as Galbraith's creature, was susceptible to democratic political pressure and labor organizing — it was embedded in institutional life in ways that made it reachable. Cloud capital, on *Technofeudalism*'s own account, is not embedded in institutional life in the same way — it operates *outside* conventional market and political structures. The very argument that makes cloud capital theoretically distinctive also makes *Another Now*'s prescriptions theoretically inadequate to it. The argument is committed to this conclusion but never states it.

### The Surplus Recycling Mechanism — Its Presence and Absence

The 'surplus recycling mechanism' vocabulary, central to *The Global Minotaur* and deployed extensively in *And the Weak Suffer What They Must?*, is strikingly absent from *Another Now*. The novel's alternative history describes the 2008 crisis as the moment of divergence — the point at which the OC rebellion succeeded in the Other Now while capitalism consolidated in Our Now. But the *mechanism* by which the post-1971 recycling apparatus shaped the 2008 crisis is not named or analyzed in the novel.

**What the absence implies**: The surplus recycling framework, which *Technofeudalism* imports as established background, was *not* the analytical substrate for the prescriptive program in *Another Now*. The novel's institutional designs were developed against the background of the Technostructure analysis (Galbraith) and the corporate governance critique, not against the macroeconomic surplus recycling analysis. This confirms the diagnostic/prescriptive genealogical separation: the prescriptions have a different intellectual ancestry (Galbraith, anarcho-syndicalism, Keynesian institutional design) than the diagnosis (surplus recycling mechanism, Global Minotaur, cloud capital as PMBM).

**The inferential consequence**: When *Technofeudalism* integrates both the surplus recycling diagnosis and the *Another Now* prescriptions, it is combining two frameworks that were developed independently and were not designed to fit together. The Kosmos system in *Another Now* was designed to solve trade imbalance problems (analogous to the surplus recycling deficit); the technofeudal diagnosis identifies a different problem (cloud capital's rent extraction). The Kosmos might address the Minotaur's legacy but does not specifically address cloud capital's PMBM function. The argument's prescriptive section is carrying a burden it was not engineered for.

## II. Semantic Mutations: What Changed in Transit

### The 'Techno-Feudalism' Phrase — From Description to System

The proto-coinage on p.145 of *Another Now* — "a species of techno-feudalism that made billions of people work for it for free" — is Eva's interior monologue observation about Big Tech's revenue model. It is descriptive, not analytical. It identifies a resemblance (like feudal extraction) without claiming structural homology. The phrase is not theorized, not placed in an intellectual genealogy, and is not doing load-bearing argumentative work in the novel.

**What the mutation involved**: The transit from descriptive phrase to master analytical category required: (a) the identification of structural mechanisms (PMBM, cloud rent, cloud fiefs) that justify calling the resemblance a homology; (b) the formal apparatus (falling rate of profit extension, prey-predator model); (c) the intellectual genealogy (Galbraith's Technostructure → cloud capital). None of these are present in *Another Now*. The novel contains the name; the analysis that justifies the name was constructed afterward.

**The inferential implication**: This means the name came before the argument. The word "techno-feudalism" was coined (or at least deployed) in the novel *as a feeling about* Big Tech's extractive character, and the subsequent analytical work in *Technofeudalism* was in part an effort to give this feeling theoretical grounding. This is the abductive mode Morozov's critique targets: the intuition preceded and shaped the formal apparatus rather than being derived from it.

What follows from this sequencing: the boundary between cloud capital and commodity production (the PMCP/PMBM distinction on which the entire argument pivots) was not derived from first principles but was constructed to support a prior intuition that something fundamentally different was happening with Big Tech. The boundary does important work; its validity does not depend on the order of discovery; but the order of discovery means it was selected to serve the intuition rather than to test it. This is the deepest layer of the Morozov challenge that neither prior pass nor the synthesis fully reached.

### The 'Democratic Deficit' — Intensification Without Radicalization

In *Another Now*, the democratic deficit is diagnosed and remediated simultaneously through institutional design: the Other Now's corporations (one-employee-one-share-one-vote), monetary system (PerCap with democratic oversight), and land management (County Associations, randomly selected) all extend democratic participation into economic life. The novel's democratic deficit is definitively solvable — the Other Now demonstrates that democratic governance of economic institutions is possible.

In *Technofeudalism*, the democratic deficit framing has shifted. Cloud capital's behavioral modification apparatus shapes what citizens want and how they relate to each other — which means the democratic deficit is not merely institutional (markets unaccountable to democratic pressure) but constitutive (the mechanisms of preference formation have been colonized by cloud capital). This is a qualitatively deeper democratic deficit than *Another Now* addressed.

**What follows from this semantic mutation**: If *Technofeudalism*'s democratic deficit is constitutive rather than merely institutional, then *Another Now*'s institutional prescriptions may be insufficient to address it. You cannot democratize the firm if workers' preferences about governance have been shaped by behavioral modification to align with cloudalist interests. You cannot establish Citizens' Juries if jurors' deliberative capacities have been modified by the same apparatus they are supposed to oversee. The deeper democratic deficit implies that institutional reform must be preceded by something like consciousness-raising — a prior project that *Another Now*'s prescriptions do not address.

The argument is committed to this implication but never states it. Instead, *Technofeudalism* presents the *Another Now* prescriptions as the alternative to the deeper democratic deficit it has diagnosed, without explaining why institutional designs adequate to a shallower problem would work against a deeper one.

## III. Appropriation Patterns: What Was Borrowed and How

### The Wormhole Fiction as Rhetorical Laundering — Inferential Consequences

The wormhole device in *Another Now* converts speculative prescriptions into discovered alternatives. The institutions of the Other Now are *reported from* rather than *proposed for* — they are empirical descriptions of a functioning society, not untested proposals. This gives them an epistemic status in the novel (documented alternatives) that they cannot have in *Technofeudalism* (acknowledged proposals).

**What must follow from the loss of this epistemic status**: When *Technofeudalism* cites *Another Now* for the "detailed blueprint," it is referring readers to a source where the blueprint's viability was claimed via fictional discovery — the wormhole proved the Other Now works. In the non-fiction context, this rhetorical ground is unavailable. The citation "see *Another Now* for the detailed blueprint" is asking readers to accept as a prescriptive proposal what in the novel was presented as documented fact. This is a category shift that the argument does not acknowledge.

The inferential consequence: the strongest argument for the prescriptions' viability in *Another Now* was the wormhole fiction itself. The 2023 book cannot claim that PerCap or Kosmos has been proven to work — only that they were *imagined to work* in the novel. The prescriptions have been downgraded epistemically by the transition from fiction to non-fiction, and this downgrade is concealed by treating the novel as a source of institutional designs rather than as a source of speculative proposals presented through fictional laundering.

**What the argument is committed to without saying so**: that the prescriptions' plausibility rests on something other than the fictional proof-of-concept. But neither *Another Now* nor *Technofeudalism* provides that other foundation. The institutional designs are reasonable and interesting, but their claim to viability in the 2023 book is parasitic on the epistemic status the 2020 novel created through fiction — and that status cannot be inherited.

### The Iris Voice Appropriation — What Was Suppressed and Why

The prior passes have identified Iris's rejection of the Other Now as the most radical internal critique of the prescriptions. What the inferential analysis adds:

**If Iris is right that democratized markets perpetuate the logic of conditional exchange and foreclose more radical transformation, then the argument in *Technofeudalism* is committed to a position it never defends: that market socialism is not merely insufficient but actively harmful to the project of transcending capitalism.**

Iris says: "I prefer to stay in Our horrid Now than live in a much better version of it that only makes the prospect of a Star Trek communism feel further away." This is a specific argument about reformist solutions entrenching the problem they partially address — the classic left critique of social democracy. Applied to *Technofeudalism*'s prescriptions: if democratized market institutions stabilize market exchange rather than transcending it, they may make the transition to genuinely post-market society *harder* rather than easier.

*Technofeudalism* never engages this argument. Its prescriptive program is exactly what Iris rejects — markets without capitalism, where the logic of conditional exchange is preserved even as corporate governance is democratized. The argument is committed to a position (that market socialism is adequate to the task) that its own prior work — through Iris's character — challenged with full dialectical force.

**Following the logic**: if Iris's critique is unanswerable (and Varoufakis never answers it in *Technofeudalism*), then the prescriptive program is vulnerable to the charge that it is a stabilization measure rather than a transcendence measure. The argument for technofeudalism as a qualitative break from capitalism, combined with market-socialism prescriptions that preserve the logic of exchange, commits the argument to an inconsistency: diagnosing a rupture that requires revolutionary response, then prescribing a reform that Iris-level analysis identifies as foreclosing revolutionary possibility.

## IV. Distortion Map: How Ideas Were Warped in Transit

### The 'Sovereignty of Good' Distortion

Iris's political philosophy in *Another Now* — freedom *from* the market rather than *freer* markets; unconditional collaboration rather than conditional exchange; the sovereignty of good rather than exchange value — is the most radical position in the novel and the one most clearly left behind by *Technofeudalism*'s prescriptions.

The distortion in transit: the novel presents Iris's position as a live intellectual option that Varoufakis (through the narrator Yango) acknowledges with respect even while not fully endorsing. The novel holds the tension. *Technofeudalism* resolves the tension by presenting the Another Now prescriptions without the voice that challenged them from the left.

**What the distortion implies about the argument's actual commitments**: *Technofeudalism* presents itself as a radical critique of capitalism but its prescriptions are market-socialist at most. The gap between the radicalism of the diagnosis (capitalism is dead, cloud capital dominates, social democracy is structurally impossible) and the modesty of the prescription (democratize firms, establish PerCap, implement Kosmos) would be more visible if Iris's critique were included. Her suppression is not accidental — it is necessary to maintain the argument's appearance of consistency between its radical diagnosis and its reform-level prescriptions.

**The inferential chain**: Radical diagnosis → modest reform prescriptions → apparent inconsistency → requires suppression of the voice that names the inconsistency. Iris's absence from *Technofeudalism* is not coincidental but structurally necessary given the argument's architecture.

### The OC Rebellion's Institutional Transformation Assumption

In *Another Now* Chapter 4, the OC rebellion succeeds by transforming existing political institutions. The text is explicit (p.83): "Congress was forced to accommodate citizens' assemblies, as were the Houses of Parliament in the United Kingdom. In China, changes to corporate law were presented as the logical extension to Deng Xiaoping's break with Maoism." The OC rebellion works *through* existing institutions, transforming them from within.

*Technofeudalism* argues that social democracy is *structurally impossible* — that cloud capital has captured the state and that existing institutions cannot be reformed to address the technofeudal condition.

**This is a silent position revision**: the novel assumed institutional transformation through collective action was achievable; the 2023 book argues it is structurally blocked. The prescriptions were designed for the institutional environment described in the novel (transformable through pressure) but are being applied to the institutional environment diagnosed in the book (resistant to transformation by structural necessity).

**What must follow if both positions are held simultaneously**: either the institutional transformation in *Another Now* was already impossible (which would mean the novel's alternative history was not merely speculative but incoherent), or the structural impossibility of social democracy is overstated in *Technofeudalism* (which would mean cloud mobilization might work through mechanisms similar to the OC rebellion). The argument cannot coherently hold both positions — the novel's success story and the book's structural pessimism are incompatible. The incompatibility is never acknowledged.

## V. Recombination: New Combinations of Old Ideas

### The Most Important Recombination: Galbraith + Marx + Keynes + *Another Now*

The argument in *Technofeudalism* combines: Galbraith's Technostructure (provides the historical trajectory through which cloud capital emerged), Marx's value theory and falling rate of profit (provides the formal basis for the PMBM/PMCP distinction), Keynes's aggregate demand framework (provides the macroeconomic account of how central bank money replaced profit), and *Another Now*'s institutional designs (provides the prescriptive program). These four elements have different intellectual genealogies and were developed for different purposes.

**The recombination produces genuine novelty**: the specific claim that cloud capital automated the Technostructure's behavioral modification functions and thereby superseded capitalism is not derivable from any of the four sources independently. It requires the combination. In this specific sense, *Technofeudalism* represents genuine intellectual innovation — not merely synthesis but the generation of a new claim that none of the source frameworks could produce alone.

**But the recombination introduces tensions that are not resolved**: (a) Galbraith was not a Marxist and his technostructure analysis does not require value theory — combining it with Marx creates tensions about whether the central analytical categories are market-institutional (Galbraith) or value-theoretic (Marx); (b) the Keynesian aggregate demand framework is agnostic about the source of income (rents vs. profits) in ways that tension with the Marxist emphasis on the distinction between them; (c) *Another Now*'s institutional designs were calibrated for a Galbraithian diagnosis (defeating the Technostructure) and do not fit cleanly with the Marxist diagnosis (cloud capital's structural supersession of capitalism).

**The inferential consequence**: the argument that presents itself as a unified Marxist analysis is actually a bricolage of incompatible frameworks whose incompatibilities are managed rather than resolved. The 2026 article's formalization (the Goodwin model, the PMBM/PMCP equations) attempts to unify the Marxist components, but the Galbraithian and Keynesian elements are still present as undischarged assumptions. The recombination is creative and productive, but it is not the unified theoretical edifice the formal apparatus suggests.

### The Democratic Deficit + PMBM Recombination — What New Tensions This Creates

*Another Now*'s democratic deficit analysis (extending democracy into economic life through institutional design) is combined with *Technofeudalism*'s PMBM analysis (cloud capital's behavioral modification constitutively shapes what citizens want). The combination creates a new analytical claim not present in either source: that cloud capital's democratic deficit is not merely about exclusion from economic governance but about the modification of democratic subjectivity itself.

**What new tensions this introduces**: if PMBM machines modify what citizens want, then Citizens' Juries whose members have been subjected to behavioral modification may not exercise the democratic judgment the institutional design presupposes. The jury selection mechanism (*Another Now* p.52: "randomness is the great ally against tyranny") assumes that randomly selected citizens have independent judgment to exercise. But *Technofeudalism*'s PMBM analysis implies that random selection of citizens who have been behaviorally modified by cloud capital is selection from a pool of modified preferences, not independent judgment.

The recombination thus creates an internal incoherence: the prescriptive apparatus assumes democratic subjectivity that the diagnostic apparatus shows has been colonized. The argument is committed to both positions without acknowledging the conflict.

## VI. Acknowledgment Status: The Politics of Self-Citation

### What Is Cited and Why

*Technofeudalism* cites *Another Now* explicitly in Chapter 7 for the "detailed blueprint." This is an acknowledgment that establishes continuity and authority: the prescriptions are presented as part of a sustained intellectual program, not ad hoc responses to the technofeudal diagnosis.

**What the acknowledgment performs**: it frames the prescriptions as the *origin* from which *Technofeudalism* developed rather than as pre-existing material that was incorporated into a new framework. The citation presents the intellectual sequence as: here is the blueprint I developed in *Another Now*, now justified by the technofeudal analysis. This is the opposite of the actual sequence (blueprint developed before the analysis) presented as if the sequence were the other way around.

**The inferential consequence**: the citation is technically honest (it acknowledges the prior source) but rhetorically misleading (it implies that the prior source was developed as a response to the technofeudal problem rather than as a response to a different problem). A fully honest acknowledgment would say: "I developed these prescriptions in *Another Now* for a different diagnosis (defeating the Technostructure through the OC rebellion), and I am now applying them to a different diagnosis (cloud capital's structural supersession of capitalism). I believe they are adequate to both problems, but I acknowledge they were not designed for the second." This acknowledgment is never made.

### What Is Not Cited and Why

**The semantic migration of the Technostructure** from contemporary enemy (*Another Now*) to historical antecedent (*Technofeudalism*) is not acknowledged. This is the most significant silent revision. The argument that cloud capital *superseded* the Technostructure — the conceptual advance between 2020 and 2023 — implies that the enemy Varoufakis was fighting in *Another Now* had already been defeated by the time he wrote *Technofeudalism*. This would mean the OC rebellion in the novel was solving the wrong problem even in its fictional success.

**The Iris-voice critique** of the prescriptions as insufficiently radical is not cited. This omission serves the argument's presentation of the prescriptions as adequate alternatives rather than as market-stabilization measures that Varoufakis's own prior work challenged.

**The 'techno-feudalism' proto-coinage** in *Another Now* (p.145) is not acknowledged as such. *Technofeudalism*'s "Influences" section mentions that the concept crystallized "by 2018" in papers and talks, without noting that the *word* appeared in the 2020 novel as a descriptive phrase before the analytical apparatus existed. This omission allows the word's appearance to seem analytically motivated (as the output of developing the concept) rather than intuitionally preceding the analysis.

**The logical conclusion of the omission pattern**: what is cited is what *Technofeudalism* wants to inherit (the prescriptive program, the institutional designs, the political vision); what is omitted is what would complicate the argument's coherence (the Iris-voice critique, the Technostructure's semantic migration, the proto-coinage as intuition-preceding-analysis). The acknowledgment pattern is selective in a way that serves the argument's rhetorical goals while suppressing its conceptual complications.

# Concept Evolution Analysis: *Another Now* (2020) as Prior Work

## Opening Archaeological Survey

*Another Now* presents an unusual analytical situation: it is a novel — science fiction framed as speculative memoir — yet it is doing serious theoretical and political-economic work that directly feeds into Varoufakis's later analytical texts. The genealogical classification passes have already established its status as a *direct precursor* in the strongest sense: the prescriptive program of *Technofeudalism* was workshopped in novelistic form three years before the diagnostic framework was articulated. What this concept evolution pass must do is stay *with the text itself* as prior work and trace what happens to its vocabulary, concepts, methods, and framings when tracked against the current work's profile. I am not summarizing prior analyses; I am reading *Another Now* as archaeological site.

## I. Vocabulary Evolution: Term-by-Term Archaeology

### "Techno-feudalism" / "Technofeudalism"

The word first appears on page 145 of *Another Now*, spoken (in internal monologue attribution) by Eva: "That was back in 2019. By 2025, Eva had become convinced that no self-respecting liberal could condone big tech's mass manipulation techniques nor defend its gains as a fair reward for entrepreneurship. Its returns were only made possible by a species of techno-feudalism that made billions of people work for it for free."

This is a *descriptive phrase*, not yet a systematic theoretical category. "A species of techno-feudalism" — the indefinite article and the qualifier "species" mark it as a characterization rather than a coined term. Compare the 2023 *Technofeudalism* book, where the word functions as the master concept organizing the entire analytical apparatus, with a full chapter ("What's in a Word?") defending its adoption over alternatives.

The semantic drift is striking: from casual analytical characterization of big tech's labor extraction model *within* the novel's future (set in 2025-2036), the term migrates to become the *title* and organizing concept of a subsequent non-fiction work. What was a novelist's shorthand for a specific mechanism (unpaid data labor = feudal-like extraction) becomes, by 2023, a comprehensive framework for diagnosing capitalist supersession.

What's also interesting is the *mechanism* in the 2020 passage: techno-feudalism makes "billions of people work for it for free." The emphasis is specifically on unpaid labor, which precisely anticipates the "cloud serf" concept in the 2023 book. The concept's initial scope was narrower — it named the specific mechanism of unpaid reproductive labor in social media — before expanding in 2023 to name an entire mode of production.

### "Corpo-Syndicalism"

This coined term names an entire chapter (Chapter 3) and the institutional design centerpiece of the Other Now's economy. It appears nowhere in *Technofeudalism* under this name. However, the institutional content — one-employee-one-share-one-vote corporate governance, democratic bonus allocation via merit points, flat management with spontaneous order — travels wholesale into *Technofeudalism* Chapter 7 under different vocabulary: "democratized companies," "another now" (the institutional blueprint reference).

The term itself has undergone what we might call **vocabulary simplification**: the specific coinage "corpo-syndicalism" gives way to the vaguer reference "another now" as the shorthand for the prescriptive vision. The conceptual content is identical; the terminological vehicle has been exchanged. This suggests Varoufakis decided against importing the specific compound noun into the analytical text — perhaps because "syndicalism" carries specific historical connotations (anarcho-syndicalism) that he wanted to either invoke or avoid more carefully, or because the 2023 book needed more accessible vocabulary.

The internal political debate within *Another Now* about corpo-syndicalism is revealing. Eva objects that flat management produces hidden hierarchies, that the ban on tradable shares violates liberty, that the Socialworthiness Index creates tyranny of community judgment. Iris objects that even the Other Now's markets reproduce patriarchal structures. Costa defends the synthesis. These debates *do not* appear in *Technofeudalism* as debates — they are resolved in advance. The novel's dialectical exploration of the prescriptions' problems is absent from the later non-fiction text, where they are presented as solutions without significant qualification.

### "Socialworthiness" / "Socialworthiness Index"

This coined term appears in *Another Now* (pages 61-64) and is transported essentially unchanged into *Technofeudalism*. It names the mechanism by which Citizens' Juries assess corporate conduct and assign a social rating that can threaten deregistration. The term is descriptive-normative: "socialworthiness" parallels "creditworthiness" but substitutes community-social standards for financial-credit standards. 

The concept has undergone minimal semantic drift between 2020 and 2023. It is a stable term that moves across texts without significant reconceptualization. The main difference: in *Another Now*, the Socialworthiness Index is embedded in a detailed sociological account of how it functions in daily life — how people check company ratings before collaborating, how they spill over into personal spheres. In *Technofeudalism*, it appears as a bare institutional design feature. The experiential texture of the concept has been stripped away in translation from fiction to analysis.

### "Personal Capital" / "PerCap"

The term "Personal Capital" (PerCap) and its tripartite structure (Legacy, Dividend, Accumulation) appear in *Another Now* Chapter 3 with remarkable technical specificity. The narrative explains the mechanism in detail: corporate revenues are taxed at 5% to fund Dividend and Legacy accounts; each employee's income goes into Accumulation; Legacy is the "trust fund for every baby" endowed by the state at birth; Dividend provides monthly income as a universal right to society's capital returns.

In *Technofeudalism*, these same concepts appear under slightly different vocabulary: "PerCap" appears by name, "central bank digital wallet" is introduced as an alternative formulation, and "universal basic dividend" replaces the specific tripartite structure in some contexts. The vocabulary has been *simplified* for a non-fiction analytical context — the technical specificity of the novel's institutional design is either cited back to *Another Now* or compressed into general principles.

What's analytically interesting is the origin story embedded in *Another Now*. PerCap is presented as having emerged *historically* in the Other Now from the OC rebellion's transformation of the Federal Reserve and central banking. This gives the prescription a fictional genealogy: it wasn't invented by Varoufakis but discovered by him through the narrative of the Other Now's development. The novel performs the labor of legitimation that the non-fiction text then inherits.

### "Technostructure" (Galbraith's term, Varoufakis's deployment)

Page 79 contains the passage already flagged in prior passes as genealogically decisive. Costa/Kosti refers to the OC rebel network as "techno-syndicalism — adapting a term coined in the mid-1960s by John Kenneth Galbraith, an eminent twentieth-century American economist who referred to capitalism's network of power, its agglomeration of mega-firms and mega-banks, as the Technostructure."

In 2020, the Technostructure is named as the *contemporary enemy* that the OC rebels are fighting. The novel's alternative history (Other Now) features the OC rebellion *successfully defeating* the Technostructure between 2008 and 2011. In 2023's *Technofeudalism*, the Technostructure has been repositioned as a *historical precursor that cloud capital superseded* — it was the governing mechanism of the post-war economy, but cloud capital's emergence represents something categorically new that automated the Technostructure's two behavioral modification functions.

This is a genuine **conceptual development** between 2020 and 2023, not merely terminological drift. In 2020, Galbraith's Technostructure = current form of capitalism = what needs to be defeated. In 2023, Galbraith's Technostructure = historical stage of capitalism = already superseded by cloud capital. The 2020 novel was written before Varoufakis developed the argument that Big Tech represented something qualitatively beyond the Technostructure; by 2023, the conceptual advance had been made. This tracks the precise moment of intellectual development: the Technostructure-to-cloud-capital conceptual move was made *between* 2020 and 2023.

### "Experiential Value" vs. "Exchange Value"

The distinction appears in *Another Now* at page 148-149, in the narrator's analysis of the Other Now's economics: "Instead of judging something's worth by its exchange value – what it would fetch in return for something else – the Other Now judged worth according to experiential value – the benefit the thing brought to the person who used it... Exchange value liberated itself from the hegemony of exchange value, the less meaningful or relevant GDP would be."

The vocabulary here is stable and already mature — "experiential value" and "exchange value" appear as settled analytical tools, not as innovations being introduced for the first time. This suggests the vocabulary was already in place in Varoufakis's analytical toolkit before *Another Now* was written (possibly established in *Talking to My Daughter*, 2017). What *Another Now* adds is the application of the distinction to GDP measurement and the claim that post-capitalism frees experiential value from exchange value's hegemony.

In *Technofeudalism*, this distinction is foundational and formally theorized in the Appendix, where "experiential value" appears as Varoufakis's deliberate substitution for Marx's "use value." The evolution: from applied distinction in the novel (where it characterizes the Other Now's economics) to foundational analytical category in the non-fiction (where it grounds the labor theory of value). The concept has been elevated from a sociological description to a theoretical foundation.

### "Crowdshorters," "Bladerunners," "Solsourcers," "Flying Pickets," "Wikiblowers," "Infiltrators"

These are *Another Now*-specific coinages for the various rebel groups in the Other Now's OC rebellion. They are rich, inventive, and precisely characterized — each group has a distinct strategy, a distinct class composition, and a distinct relationship to capitalist institutions.

Crucially: *none of these terms appear in Technofeudalism*. They were not imported. What *was* imported is the *general tactical logic* — the "Bladerunners' Days of Inaction" becomes "cloud mobilization" in *Technofeudalism*, but the specific coinage is replaced by a generic analytical term.

This selective non-importation is analytically significant. It suggests Varoufakis recognized that the fictional specificity of these terms would be an obstacle in analytical writing — they are too evocative of the novel's fictional world to function as analytical categories. The tactical concepts were abstracted and generalized; the fictional vocabulary was left behind. This is a clear case of **vocabulary death in translation from fiction to analysis**, where the concept survives but the term does not.

### "Kosmos"

Fully developed in *Another Now* Chapter 6 (pages 131-135) as a supranational accounting unit analogous to Keynes's Bancor. The mechanism: all international trade denominated in Ks (Kosmos units), Trade Imbalance Levy on both surplus and deficit countries, Surge Funding Levy on sudden cross-border capital flows, International Redistribution and Development Depository channeling levy proceeds to underdeveloped regions. Currency exchange rates determined by daily IMP auctions.

In *Technofeudalism*, "Kosmos" appears in Chapter 7 with identical properties. The term has traveled without semantic drift. What has changed is the *justification*: in *Another Now*, Kosmos emerges from the historical logic of the OC rebellion's institutional innovations; in *Technofeudalism*, it is prescribed as the solution to a specific diagnosed problem (inadequate surplus recycling mechanism after the Global Minotaur's death). The same prescription is being offered for different reasons, with the diagnostic framework retrofitted to justify what was already prescriptively formulated.

### "HALPEVAM," "CREST," "Cerberus," "Freedom Machine"

These are the science fiction devices central to *Another Now*'s plot — the pleasure maximizer, the subatomic memory trail, the security system, the utopian machine. They appear nowhere in *Technofeudalism* — completely absent from the later analytical work. They represent **vocabulary specific to the novel's science fiction register** that has no place in the analytical text.

However, HALPEVAM's conceptual logic — a machine that generates personalized pleasure realities — anticipates *Technofeudalism*'s account of cloud capital's behavioral modification function. The machine that offers you what you want based on your complete behavioral history is precisely what PMBM machines do. The Science fiction device was a thought experiment for the mechanism that becomes the analytical core of the later work. This is a **concept migration without vocabulary transfer**: the concept (machine that personalizes behavioral experience based on accumulated data) travels forward; the fictional vocabulary (HALPEVAM, CREST) does not.

## II. Concept Trajectory: Lifecycles and Transformations

### The "Unpaid Labor = Serfdom" Concept

**Trajectory type: EXPANSION**

In *Another Now*, the concept of users as unpaid labor appears in Eva's analysis of Facebook (page 144): "Walmart, a company renowned for its capacity to squeeze every drop of value out of its workers, pays out 40 per cent of its total revenue in wages. But Facebook pays only 1 per cent of its revenues to its employees and precisely nothing to its users!" The concept is present but stated as a factoid, not yet theorized.

In *Technofeudalism*, this same observation is the foundation for the entire "cloud serf" concept — the most theoretically developed and politically charged category in the 2023 book. What was a striking data point in the novel has become an analytically central category with its own formal apparatus (the PMBM/PMCP distinction explaining *why* cloud serf labor is unproductive yet extractive).

The expansion is significant: from empirical observation (Facebook doesn't pay users) to structural analysis (cloud capital systematically extracts unpaid labor outside any labor market as its primary accumulation mechanism). The novel planted the seed; the analytical work grew the plant.

### The Prescriptions-as-Discovery Concept

**Trajectory type: FRAMING SHIFT**

In *Another Now*, the institutional prescriptions are presented through a discovery narrative: Costa/Kosti communicates with the Other Now, and the institutional arrangements there are *discovered* — they are shown as already working in practice in an alternative reality. This framing performs a specific ideological function: it converts speculative proposals into "empirically" documented alternatives. The Other Now's corpo-syndicalism, PerCap, and Kosmos are not Varoufakis's untested ideas but descriptions of a functioning society (fictional, yes, but with the rhetorical force of existence claims).

In *Technofeudalism*, the same prescriptions appear under a different framing: they are cited as a blueprint from *Another Now* (the book, now named as prior work). The framing has shifted from *discovery* (we learned this from the Other Now) to *proposal* (I argued for this in a prior book). The epistemic status has been clarified — these are admitted to be proposals — but the rhetorical authority of the discovery framing has been sacrificed.

This framing shift represents a loss of rhetorical power that the prior analyses identified as the "Victorian melodrama" problem in miniature: the novel did ideological work through the discovery frame that the non-fiction text cannot do through direct proposal.

### The "Market Without Capitalism" Concept

**Trajectory type: NARROWING AND FORMALIZATION**

In *Another Now*, the concept of "markets without capitalism" is the novel's central theoretical claim — the Other Now has competitive product markets, free exchange, and efficient price mechanisms, but without tradable shares, commercial banks, or private land ownership. Eva's eventual conversion is specifically to this concept: "a proper market revival requires the end of capitalism" (page 150).

The concept functions in the novel as a *resolution of the capitalism/markets debate* — it shows that markets and capitalism are separable, that the left need not be anti-market to be anti-capitalist.

In *Technofeudalism*, "markets without capitalism" appears not as a positive vision but as a brief reference in Chapter 7 to the *Another Now* blueprint. The concept has been *narrowed*: it no longer does the philosophical work of resolving the capitalism/markets debate; it simply names the prescriptive alternative. What was the novel's central theoretical contribution has become a citation in the non-fiction work.

More significantly, *Technofeudalism* has a different relationship to markets than *Another Now* does. The 2023 book mourns the replacement of markets by cloud fiefs and argues that markets are better than what has replaced them. This is the "market defense" that the prior synthesis analysis identified as an unacknowledged normative commitment. *Another Now* does not make this argument — it argues that markets can survive and improve after capitalism's abolition. The two texts have subtly different normative stances toward markets, and tracking this difference reveals that *Technofeudalism* has developed a more defensive relationship to markets than the 2020 novel anticipated.

### The Iris Character and the Radical Market Critique

**Trajectory type: SUPPRESSION**

This is the most analytically interesting concept trajectory, and the most revealing about what was *not* inherited from the prior work.

In *Another Now*, Iris represents the position that even the Other Now's post-capitalist society is insufficiently radical because it preserves market exchange. Her final position, stated clearly: "I prefer to stay in Our horrid Now than live in a much better version of it that only makes the prospect of a Star Trek communism feel further away." She argues that democratized markets are better than capitalist markets but remain "conditioned exchange" that prevents genuine freedom — freedom *from* markets rather than *of* markets.

This is the strongest internal critique of the *Another Now* prescriptions. Iris sees market socialism as a system that stabilizes market exchange and thereby forecloses the more radical transformation toward gift economy, unconditional collaboration, and the "sovereignty of good" (a concept she develops from Iris Murdoch's ethics).

In *Technofeudalism*, this critique is almost entirely absent. The book does not acknowledge the possibility that market socialism is an insufficient response to technofeudalism — it presents the Another Now blueprint as the alternative without engaging the Iris-level objection that the alternative is too market-friendly.

The concept has been **suppressed in translation**. The most radical voice in the prior work was silenced in the later non-fiction. This is genealogically significant: it reveals that Varoufakis chose not to inherit the most challenging internal criticism of his own prescriptions, even though he had articulated it himself in the prior work.

## III. Methodology Evolution: How the Argument Proceeds

### From Socratic Dialogue to Biographical Essay

The *Another Now* structure is fundamentally dialogic. Three characters with different political and intellectual commitments debate the Other Now's institutions across multiple chapters. No position is definitively established — Iris remains unconvinced to the end; Eva converts but with specific reservations; Costa is enthusiastic but troubled by his own technological creation's dangers.

This dialogic method allows multiple positions to be held in tension, allows objections to be stated at full strength, and allows the prescriptions to be stress-tested through fictional opposition.

*Technofeudalism* replaces this with the biographical essay — a letter from son to father, where the analytical voice is singular, authoritative, and persuasive rather than dialogic. The same prescriptions are presented without the dialogic stress-testing that *Another Now* subjected them to.

The methodological shift has a specific intellectual cost: the strongest objections (Iris's market critique, Eva's share-trading defense) are resolved in advance rather than engaged. The analytical text is more authoritative and less exploratory than the fictional text it drew from. Morozov's "Victorian melodrama" charge misidentifies the problem — the issue is not that the analysis is too narrative but that the narrative in *Another Now* was more dialectically honest than the analysis in *Technofeudalism*.

### From Fiction-as-Thought-Experiment to Analysis-as-Argument

*Another Now* is explicitly a thought experiment in the Euler/Gauss tradition (explained by Eva: "accepting incredible assumptions can be a gateway to enlightenment"). The wormhole and the Other Now are premises adopted for the sake of argument, not truth claims. This is the methodological framework: suspend disbelief → explore institutional possibilities → learn what might be realizable.

*Technofeudalism* is not a thought experiment but an analytical argument making truth claims about the present. The methodological shift from fictional thought experiment to analytical diagnosis is the most fundamental change between the two texts. What was genuinely hypothetical in 2020 ("what if capitalism died?") is asserted as historical fact in 2023 ("capitalism is now dead").

The novel's epistemic honesty (these are thought experiments in a fictional world) has been replaced with analytical confidence that the prior analyses identified as an overstatement. The methodological shift from thought experiment to assertion is where the argument becomes most epistemically strained.

## IV. Metaphor Evolution: Images That Structure Thought

### The TATIANA/TINA Opposition

"There Is No Alternative" (TINA, Thatcher's phrase) vs. "That Astonishingly There Is AN Alternative" (TATIANA) appears on page 65 of *Another Now* as Costa's message to Iris: "TINA was a lie. TATIANA lives. I have the proof." This is a clever rhetorical inversion that captures the novel's entire political purpose: demonstrating that alternatives exist.

The TATIANA concept does not appear by name in *Technofeudalism*, but its function is performed by the "Another Now" reference throughout Chapter 7. The novel's rhetorical slogan has been absorbed into the structural argument.

### The Wormhole/Other Now Metaphor

*Another Now*'s central narrative device — the wormhole through which messages from an alternative present can be received — is a brilliant metaphorical structure for "thought experiment" made almost literal. It is science-fictional apparatus that does the rhetorical work of making alternatives feel *documented* rather than merely *imagined*.

This metaphor appears nowhere in *Technofeudalism*. The citation back to *Another Now* (the book) in Chapter 7 performs a related but diminished function: "I developed these ideas in *Another Now*" is a bibliographic reference, not a metaphor. The wormhole's rhetorical force — the sense that the alternative actually exists, that we are *receiving reports* from it — is completely absent from the analytical text.

The metaphor's death in translation is significant. The wormhole was doing rhetorical work (alternatives are real, not merely imagined) that the analytical citation cannot replicate. The prior work's most powerful rhetorical device was untranslatable into the later analytical register.

### The "Diary" and "Dispatches" Metaphor

This is the most politically significant silent revision: the radical feminist critique that Varoufakis articulated through Iris in 2020 is not inherited by the 2023 analytical text.

In *Technofeudalism*, this rhetorical transformation is impossible; the novel's discovery frame is replaced by a bibliographic citation ("I argued for this in *Another Now*"). The prescriptions lose their claimed empirical grounding when they move from fictional documentation to acknowledged proposal.

**Framing** tells us that the epistemic status of the prescriptions undergoes a fundamental shift: from "we know these work because we have received reports from the Other Now" (subjunctive-conditional elevated to quasi-documentary status by the wormhole fiction) to "these are institutional proposals I developed in a prior book" (explicit proposal status without the fictional shield). This is actually an epistemic improvement—the 2023 book is more honest about what the prescriptions are—but it is simultaneously a rhetorical diminishment, because the novel's wormhole fiction was providing something that honest non-fiction cannot replicate.

When all four dimensions are held together, the paradox emerges with full force: the prescription preceded the diagnosis, the prescriptions were calibrated for a problem the novel identified (defeating the Technostructure), and when the diagnosis changed (cloud capital superseded the Technostructure), the prescriptions were not recalibrated. They were transported intact from a 2020 context into a 2023 context that presents a different diagnostic challenge, and the fact of this transport is visible across all four dimensions simultaneously.

## The Technostructure Migration: A Conceptual Development Caught Between Texts

The most revealing single passage in *Another Now* for understanding what changed between 2020 and 2023 appears on page 79. Kosti describes the OC rebel network as "techno-syndicalism—adapting a term coined in the mid-1960s by John Kenneth Galbraith, an eminent twentieth-century American economist who referred to capitalism's network of power, its agglomeration of mega-firms and mega-banks, as the Technostructure." Costa's reaction is significant: "he had never met anyone else familiar with that term. And then he remembered that until 2008 he and Kosti had been the same person."

In *Another Now* (2020), Galbraith's Technostructure is the *contemporary enemy* the OC rebels are fighting. It is the present-tense name for capitalism's governing power structure, and the prescriptions (corpo-syndicalism, PerCap, Kosmos, PASS) are calibrated to defeat it. In *Technofeudalism* (2023), the same Technostructure has been repositioned as a *historical precursor that cloud capital already superseded*—it was the governing mechanism of the post-war economy, but cloud capital's emergence automated both of its behavioral modification functions (Labor Command and Consumer Command service sectors) and rendered it obsolete.

This migration of the Technostructure from contemporary enemy to historical antecedent is the conceptual advance that occurred between the writing of *Another Now* and the writing of *Technofeudalism*. It tracks across all four dimensions in ways that reveal the character of the intellectual development:

The **vocabulary dimension** shows this as: in *Another Now*, the term "technostructure" is current analytical vocabulary (what we're fighting); in *Technofeudalism*, it is historical vocabulary (what cloud capital replaced). The same word, the same Galbraithian genealogy, but an entirely different temporal position.

The **methodology dimension** shows this as: in *Another Now*, the OC rebellion can succeed by defeating the Technostructure through institutional transformation from within existing political structures (page 83: "Congress was forced to accommodate citizens' assemblies... In China, changes to corporate law were presented as the logical extension to Deng Xiaoping's break with Maoism"). This success is possible because the Technostructure, as the enemy, can be defeated through collective action and institutional design. In *Technofeudalism*, cloud capital cannot be defeated this way—it is immune to the regulatory tools that worked against the Technostructure—and social democracy is therefore structurally impossible. The methodological shift from institutional reform to revolutionary coalition reflects a genuine change in what the enemy is understood to be.

The **metaphor dimension** shows this as: in *Another Now*, the Technostructure metaphor organizes the political analysis—capitalism is structured like a network of power that can be disrupted. In *Technofeudalism*, cloud capital metaphors (cloud fiefs, cloud serfs, the behavioral modification machine) replace the network-of-power metaphor with a fief-extraction metaphor that implies different political responses. The metaphors changed because the diagnosed enemy changed.

The **framing dimension** shows this as: in *Another Now*, the question is "how do we defeat the Technostructure?" and the answer is institutional reform through collective action. In *Technofeudalism*, the question is "what superseded capitalism?" and the Technostructure appears in the answer as a historical stepping stone, not the terminal form. The framing has shifted from prescriptive-political (what should we do?) to diagnostic-analytical (what has happened to us?).

What makes this cross-dimensional pattern so analytically rich is that it shows a genuine conceptual development—the recognition that Big Tech represents something qualitatively beyond the Technostructure, not its continuation—caught between the two texts. *Another Now* was written before Varoufakis made this conceptual advance; *Technofeudalism* was written after. The prior work preserves the last moment before the advance; the current work reflects the advance without fully acknowledging its implications for the prescriptions that were developed under the prior understanding.

## The Four Dimensions as Independent Axes of Change

The dimensional comparison matrix reveals that the four dimensions do not all change in the same direction or at the same rate. Mapping them against each other:

**Vocabulary** changes most dramatically and cleanly: the prescriptive vocabulary (PerCap, Kosmos, PASS) carries forward essentially unchanged, while the diagnostic vocabulary (cloud capital, PMBM, technofeudalism) is entirely new. This makes vocabulary the clearest indicator of the prescription-precedes-diagnosis reversal.

**Methodology** changes in a specific way that is neither simple continuity nor simple break. The Socratic-dialogue method of *Another Now*—in which the strongest objections to the prescriptions are stated at full force—is abandoned in *Technofeudalism*'s biographical-essay format. This is a methodological regression in one dimension (dialectical honesty about the prescriptions' weaknesses) while being a methodological advance in another (the biographical father-letter format enables different kinds of claims about lived experience and historical change). The regression and advance are decoupled: the novel was more dialectically honest about internal critique; the analytical book is more rigorous about historical causation.

**Metaphor** changes most radically: the wormhole fiction, the Other Now discovery frame, the HALPEVAM and CREST science-fiction apparatus—none of this survives into *Technofeudalism*. These were the methodological innovations of the novel, and they were doing work (making speculative proposals seem like documented alternatives) that is irreproducible in analytical prose. The metaphors that do survive are the institutional ones (PerCap, Kosmos), which were always descriptive rather than rhetorical.

**Framing** changes in a direction that is harder to characterize than the others. The novel's framing is fundamentally *conditional-speculative*: "what if the OC rebellion had succeeded?" The analytical book's framing is *diagnostic-declarative*: "capitalism is now dead." The movement from conditional to declarative is the most consequential framing change, and it represents the prescriptive-precedes-diagnostic reversal in its most acute form: the diagnosis was developed to give declarative force to prescriptions that were already in place.

The key synthetic insight from mapping these four changes against each other: **vocabulary and framing are coupled** (the introduction of the diagnostic vocabulary enables the declarative framing), **methodology and metaphor are decoupled** (the methodological shift from dialogue to essay and the metaphorical shift from wormhole to citation are independent), and **prescriptive vocabulary is stable across all dimensions** while **diagnostic vocabulary is entirely new** to the current work. This coupling and decoupling pattern reveals that the argument in *Technofeudalism* is genuinely new in its diagnosis but genuinely old in its prescription, and the four dimensions of change reflect this asymmetry: where the diagnosis changed, vocabulary and framing changed together; where the prescription was merely inherited, vocabulary stability across dimensions confirms the inheritance.

## The Iris Voice: The Suppression That Explains Everything

Among the most analytically consequential findings in the full textual examination is what *Another Now* contains that *Technofeudalism* does not: the Iris voice. Iris—radical feminist, consummate syndicalist, critical of social democracy's failures, ultimately refusal of the Other Now—articulates the strongest internal critique of the entire prescriptive program. Her final position is the most radical in the novel: "I prefer to stay in Our horrid Now than live in a much better version of it that only makes the prospect of a Star Trek communism feel further away."

What Iris objects to is not technofeudalism—she has no framework for that concept, which doesn't exist yet in 2020—but market socialism itself. She sees the Other Now's democratized markets as a stabilization of the market system that forecloses more radical transformation toward what she calls "the sovereignty of good": unconditional collaboration, gift economy, freedom *from* markets rather than *freer* markets. Her objection is that even the Other Now's post-capitalist institutions preserve the transactional quid pro quo mentality that market exchange instills.

This objection, voiced with full force in the novel, is entirely absent from *Technofeudalism*. The 2023 book presents the Another Now prescriptions as the alternative to technofeudalism without engaging the Iris-level critique that the alternative is too market-friendly. The three-dimensional analytical advantage of the novel—which could hold Iris's radical refusal alongside Eva's liberal accommodation and Costa's enthusiastic advocacy—has been collapsed in the non-fiction to a single affirmative voice.

Across the four dimensions, this suppression appears as:
- **Vocabulary**: Iris's vocabulary of "sovereignty of good," "unconditional exchange," "freedom from the market" does not appear in *Technofeudalism*
- **Methodology**: The dialectical testing that the novel performed—allowing the strongest objections their full force—is methodologically impossible in the biographical essay format of the analytical book
- **Metaphor**: Iris's "cockroach" metaphor for patriarchy's persistence (page 158)—"mountains move, banking becomes extinct, even capitalism dies. But patriarchy lives on like a hard-to-kill cockroach"—and the entire feminist political analysis connected to it does not appear in *Technofeudalism*
- **Framing**: The novel frames the prescriptions as dialectically contested; the analytical book frames them as the affirmative alternative

The suppression of Iris's voice is not incidental. It reveals that the most challenging internal critique of the prescriptive program—the argument that market socialism is insufficiently radical—was available to Varoufakis in 2020 and was deliberately not inherited by *Technofeudalism* in 2023. This is the silent position revision that the analytical emphasis instruction asked to flag: the prescriptions' most radical critic, voiced in the prior work at full strength, was silenced in the current work.

The structural implication is significant: if Iris is right—if democratized markets are insufficient because they preserve the logic of conditional exchange—then *Technofeudalism*'s Chapter 7 is not the radical alternative it presents itself as but a stabilization program that forecloses more transformative possibilities. The prior work contained this critique; the current work suppressed it. Whether this suppression represents intellectual progress (the arguments were settled) or intellectual regression (the objections were avoided) is the deepest question the genealogical analysis raises.

## The Wormhole as Irreplaceable Rhetorical Device

One of the most analytically interesting findings from the metaphor dimension involves what was lost—not what was gained—in the transition from fiction to analysis. The wormhole in *Another Now* is not merely a science-fiction device; it is a *rhetorical mechanism* that converts speculative proposals into documented alternatives. Because the novel's premise is that the Other Now actually exists and that Costa is receiving dispatches from it, the institutional proposals (PerCap, corpo-syndicalism, Kosmos) can be presented as descriptions of functioning arrangements rather than as untested proposals. The wormhole gives the prescriptions an epistemic status—"these work; we have documented evidence from the Other Now"—that no honest analytical text can provide.

In *Technofeudalism*, the wormhole's function is replaced by the bibliographic citation: "I developed these proposals in *Another Now*." This is epistemically more honest—it admits that the proposals are invented rather than discovered—but it is rhetorically much weaker. "I argued for this in a prior book" is not the same rhetorical force as "these institutions are functioning in an alternative society we can observe." The 2023 book inherits the prescriptions but cannot inherit the rhetorical ground on which their viability was claimed.

This metaphor change tracks with the framing change: from discovery to proposal, from documented alternatives to invented proposals. The most powerful rhetorical device in the prior work was non-transferable into the analytical register of the current work. The prior analysis passes correctly identified this as a loss of rhetorical power, but the full dimensional comparison reveals that the loss is structural, not incidental—it follows necessarily from the transition from science fiction to analytical essay, and no amount of stylistic sophistication in the analytical text can reproduce what the wormhole's discovery frame provided.

## The Democratic Deficit: More Alarming, Less Radical

The analytical emphasis instruction asks to trace the "democratic deficit" framing across the works. The term itself does not appear prominently as a coinage in *Another Now*—it appears more explicitly in *And the Weak Suffer What They Must?* and *Technofeudalism*. But the concept pervades the novel through its institutional design: all of the Other Now's institutions (one-person-one-vote corporate governance, Citizens' Juries, Citizens' Assemblies, randomized selection for public roles) are designed to extend democracy into economic life that capitalism keeps under oligarchic control.

What the dimensional comparison reveals is a framing shift in the democratic deficit analysis between 2020 and 2023 that cuts across all four dimensions:

In *Another Now*, the democratic deficit is framed as a problem with a solution: the OC rebellion achieved democratic economic governance by designing institutions that extended democratic participation into the corporate, monetary, and land spheres. The framing is positive-prescriptive: here are the institutions that overcome the democratic deficit.

In *Technofeudalism*, the democratic deficit framing becomes diagnostic-alarming: cloud capital has not merely failed to deepen democracy but is actively eliminating the conditions for meaningful democratic governance. The technofeudal order makes social democracy structurally impossible. The framing has shifted from "here is how to achieve economic democracy" to "the conditions for political democracy are being destroyed."

This shift is more alarming but, paradoxically, less radical in aspiration: the novel aimed at economic democracy extending into all spheres of life; the analytical book aims at defending the conditions for political democracy against their technofeudal erosion. The regression in ambition—from economic democratization to political democracy defense—is a framing change that carries across vocabulary (the prescriptive vocabulary of the novel aims at transformation; the diagnostic vocabulary of the 2023 book aims at explaining what was lost), methodology (the novel demonstrates how economic democracy works; the analytical book diagnoses why it's being foreclosed), and metaphor (the wormhole transports us to a better world; the central bank and stock exchange examples show a worse world emerging).

## Silent Position Revision: The Institutional Transformation Assumption

The most structurally significant silent position revision between *Another Now* and *Technofeudalism* involves the assumption about institutional transformation embedded in the OC rebellion's success. In *Another Now*, the OC rebellion succeeds by working through existing political institutions in many countries (page 83: "Congress was forced to accommodate citizens' assemblies, as were the Houses of Parliament in the United Kingdom. In China, changes to corporate law were presented as the logical extension to Deng Xiaoping's break with Maoism in the 1990s"). The novel's OC rebellion is not anti-institutional; it transforms institutions from within through collective action and legal reform.

This is inconsistent with *Technofeudalism*'s claim that social democracy is structurally impossible—that cloud capital has captured the state and that existing institutions cannot be reformed to address the technofeudal condition. The 2023 book's more pessimistic position represents a silent revision of the more optimistic assumption embedded in the 2020 novel. The revision is unacknowledged because *Technofeudalism* cites *Another Now* as the source of the prescriptions without acknowledging that those prescriptions were developed in a context where institutional transformation was assumed to be achievable through the OC rebellion's mechanisms.

This creates a genuine incoherence in the current argument's architecture: the prescriptions (developed in a context assuming institutional transformation is possible) are being used to address a diagnosis that assumes institutional transformation is structurally blocked. The prescriptions are calibrated for the wrong resistance level. The dimensional comparison makes this visible because the methodology dimension shows the OC rebellion's success as a *design feature* of the novel's thought experiment, while the framing dimension of the 2023 book presents social democracy's impossibility as a diagnostic conclusion—and these two cannot coexist without acknowledging the revision.

## The Dimensional Matrix: What the Coupling Pattern Reveals

Holding all four dimensions together in a single analytical frame:

**Where all four dimensions change together**: The Technostructure-to-cloud-capital conceptual advance is visible across all four dimensions simultaneously—the Technostructure vocabulary is historical in 2023 where it was contemporary in 2020 (vocabulary), the methodology shifts from institutional reform to coalition building (methodology), the Technostructure-as-network metaphor gives way to cloud-capital-as-fief metaphor (metaphor), and the framing shifts from "how do we defeat the Technostructure?" to "what has superseded capitalism?" (framing). When all four dimensions change together, a genuine reconceptualization is underway. This is the argument that the conceptual advance between 2020 and 2023 is real, not merely terminological.

**Where vocabulary is stable but other dimensions change**: The prescriptive vocabulary (PerCap, Kosmos, PASS) is stable across the two texts, but the methodology changes (from Socratic testing to affirmative presentation), the metaphor changes (from discovered reality to cited proposal), and the framing changes (from "these work in the Other Now" to "I proposed these in a prior book"). When vocabulary is stable but three other dimensions change, the stability is superficial—the same words are carrying different epistemic burdens, embedded in different argumentative structures, performing different rhetorical functions. The stability of prescriptive vocabulary conceals a deep structural change in the epistemic status of the prescriptions.

**Where metaphor changes but framing is relatively stable**: The wormhole metaphor is entirely absent from *Technofeudalism*, but the confidence in the prescriptions is maintained. This decoupling reveals that the wormhole was doing rhetorical work (justifying confidence in untested proposals by making them seem documented) that the analytical book compensates for through a different mechanism: the authority of the analytical framework itself, the citation back to a prior published work, and the biographical weight of the argument's personal stakes. The metaphor's absence requires compensating moves in the framing register.

The most analytically productive coupling pattern is the one that reveals the argument's deepest structural feature: prescriptive vocabulary and the Iris critique are inversely coupled across the four dimensions. Where prescriptive vocabulary is most dense and confident (Chapter 7 of *Technofeudalism*), the Iris-level critique is most completely absent. Where Iris's voice is most fully present (*Another Now* Chapter 7), the prescriptions are most dialectically contested. This inverse coupling across vocabulary and framing reveals that the prescriptions' confidence in *Technofeudalism* is purchased at the price of suppressing the most radical internal critique of those prescriptions—a critique that the prior work articulated with full dialectical force.

## What This Prior Work Uniquely Reveals

*Another Now* is the only work in Varoufakis's corpus that can reveal the prescription-precedes-diagnosis reversal, because it is the only work that contains both the complete prescriptive program (already developed) and the absence of the diagnostic framework (not yet developed). Every other prior work contributes vocabulary, methodology, metaphor, or framing to the diagnostic analysis; *Another Now* is uniquely positioned as the work where the prescriptions were finalized and the diagnosis was still absent.

This uniqueness has a specific analytical implication: the argument in *Technofeudalism* Chapter 7 is vulnerable to a criticism that could only be made with knowledge of *Another Now*—that the prescriptions are not derived from the technofeudal diagnosis but were developed for a different problem (defeating the Technostructure through the OC rebellion) and retrofitted to address a different diagnosis (cloud capital's structural dominance). The prescriptions' fit with the diagnosis is imperfect precisely because they were not designed for it. The Iris character's objection—that even post-capitalist market institutions stabilize exchange-value logic and foreclose more radical transformation—applies equally against the technofeudal diagnosis, and Varoufakis never addresses this mismatch because acknowledging it would require admitting that the prescriptions need recalibration for the new diagnosis, not just transportation from the old one.

The dimensional comparison matrix, when fully synthesized, reveals this mismatch as the argument's deepest structural feature—more fundamental than any individual commitment conflict or empirical vulnerability identified in the prior analytical passes. The prescriptions and the diagnosis have different genealogies, were developed for different problems, and are held together in *Technofeudalism* by the sheer force of Varoufakis's authorial will rather than by the logical necessity that a prescription derived from its diagnosis would have. This is what no other prior work, and no single analytical dimension alone, could reveal. The synthesis across all four dimensions of the *Another Now* comparison makes it visible as the shape of the whole.
