## Opening Orientation: What These Two Texts Are

The prior work presents a doubled object: the 2023 *Technofeudalism* book and the 2026 "Marxist Case" article, which is explicitly a *retrospective defense and formalization* of the 2023 book. This is unusual and analytically rich. The 2026 article isn't merely a continuation — it's a self-critique, a theoretical hardening, and a calibrated response to named critics (Morozov, Snow, Addison and Eisenberg). It allows us to watch Varoufakis watching himself, which reveals which concepts he considers load-bearing versus decorative, and which terms he's willing to defend versus quietly revise.

The current work's profile, as established by the prior analysis, centers on Varoufakis's coined vocabulary (surplus recycling / Global Minotaur, cloud capital, cloud rent, cloud serfs/proles, cloud fiefs, technofeudalism), his methodological signature (Keynesian aggregate demand + mythological framing), and the specific inferential commitments those concepts carry. The prior analysis has extensively mapped the *architecture* of the argument. What remains for this pass is to trace the actual textual surface of vocabulary evolution, to follow concepts through the texts with archaeological care, and to note every mutation, drift, and emergence visible in the text.

## I. Vocabulary: Birth, Death, Mutation, Persistence

### The Genealogy of "Technofeudalism" as a Term

The word itself is introduced in the book with explicit self-consciousness. Varoufakis tells us it crystallized "by 2018" in papers and talks, while the book was published in 2023. The 2026 article's Section 10 is titled "What's in a Word?" — directly echoing Chapter 5 of the book which carries the same title from Shakespeare. This terminological persistence is striking: the same philosophical defense of naming is reproduced across the two texts, but with different rhetorical functions. In the book, the naming defense is addressed to the "father" character (the biographical scaffold) and is framed as: why not call it "hyper-capitalism" or "platform capitalism"? In the 2026 article, the same defense responds to Morozov, Snow, and others by name. The vocabulary of the defense has sharpened from *narrative persuasion* to *theoretical necessity*.

The word "technofeudalism" itself has not changed, but its semantic field has. In the book it functions partly through the feudal metaphor's emotional register — serfs, overlords, vassals, fiefs all activate medieval imagery that the prior analysis called "Victorian melodrama." In the 2026 article, Varoufakis is explicitly responding to Morozov's charge that the medieval vocabulary is melodrama. His response is to formalize. He creates acronyms: PMCP, PMBM, ASRC. He introduces differential equations. He builds a four-phase model. The *feudal* vocabulary recedes slightly (it's still present but less rhetorically central), while the *analytical* vocabulary expands dramatically. The word "technofeudalism" now anchors a formal apparatus rather than a narrative atmosphere.

### "Cloud Capital" — Semantic Stability with Definitional Hardening

In the book, "cloud capital" is introduced narratively through the Alexa dialogue scene, then explained through contrasts (not a market, not standard capital), and positioned as "a produced means of behavioural modification." The 2026 article hardens this into a formal analytical category with an acronym: **PMBM** (produced means of behavioural modification) — contrasted explicitly with **PMCP** (produced means of commodity production). This PMBM/PMCP distinction is the conceptual innovation that most distinguishes the 2026 article from the 2023 book.

The distinction was *implicit* in the book — Varoufakis repeatedly says cloud capital "produces no commodities" but generates "extractive powers" — but never formalized with its own terminology. The book's vocabulary for this distinction relied on contrast: cloud capital vs. "terrestrial capital," the behavioral modification function vs. the production function. In the 2026 article, the formal acronyms crystallize this into a proper analytical category that can then be operated on (plugged into equations, used to define "unproductive" in the Marxist technical sense).

This is a vocabulary *birth* in the strict sense: PMBM and PMCP are new terms that didn't appear in the 2023 book but formalize a distinction that was already doing conceptual work there. The genealogical connection is direct but the explicit terminological vehicle is new.

### "Cloud Rent" — Persistence with Supplementary Precision

Both texts use "cloud rent" extensively. In the book, cloud rent is explained primarily through the Apple Store example (30% cut on third-party developer revenues) and the Amazon commission model. The term is contrasted with profit throughout Chapter 5 ("What's in a Word?"). In the 2026 article, cloud rent acquires a more formal character through endnote 7, which addresses the difficulty of *measuring* cloud rent and the way it's concealed in accounting (the Alexa example: sold below cost, yet generates massive rents not ascribed to Alexa by accountants). 

What's notable is the 2026 article's acknowledgment — not present in this form in the book — that "Since Ricardo (1817) introduced the distinction between profits and rents, and Baran and Sweezy (1966) the distinction between monopoly profits and monopoly surplus, the accounting profession proved neither interested nor capable of accounting for these separately." This is a subtle *concession* about the empirical measurability problem that the prior analysis identified as a structural vulnerability. The book presents cloud rent as a clear phenomenon; the 2026 article acknowledges the measurement problem directly, then deflects it by pointing to the same problem in Ricardo's account of ground rent. 

The concept has therefore undergone what we might call *defensive supplementation* — its core meaning is unchanged, but it now comes equipped with an explicit response to the "how do you measure it?" objection.

### "Cloud Serfs" and "Cloud Proles" — Terminological Stability, But...

Both terms appear prominently in both texts. But there's a subtle shift in how the *relationship* between them is presented. In the book, the distinction is primarily occupational: cloud proles are waged workers whose labor is commanded by cloud-based algorithms (Amazon warehouse workers, Uber drivers); cloud serfs are *everyone* who performs unpaid labor to reproduce cloud capital (posting on Instagram, clicking on YouTube). The distinction tracks the waged/unwaged axis.

In the 2026 article, the distinction is preserved but a new element enters: the concept of **unpaid labor directly reproducing cloud capital** receives much more formal treatment. Endnote 29 is the key site: "with every one of our posts our costs of switching away from Instagram increase. Thus, without doing anything, Meta sees its cloud capital (its power to keep us engaged so that it can modify our behaviour) accumulate in direct proportion to the unwaged labour of its users which, inavertedly [sic] increases their switching costs." 

This is a *conceptual enrichment* — the serfdom mechanism is now explained not just as "you work for free for Jeff Bezos" but through a more precise account of *switching costs* as the mechanism by which unpaid labor generates cloud capital. The switching cost mechanism was present in the book (the "hostages" passage in Chapter 7 about contacts, photos, chat histories) but it wasn't formally connected to the capital accumulation analysis. In the 2026 article it becomes the linchpin: your unpaid labor doesn't just add value to someone else's platform, it *raises your own cost of leaving*, thereby directly increasing the extractive power of cloud capital.

This is not merely a vocabulary change — it's a conceptual deepening that has implications for the political strategy analysis. If the mechanism of serfdom is the self-reinforcing accumulation of switching costs through unpaid labor, then the political strategy of "cloud mobilization" (just don't use Amazon for one day) looks even more inadequate than it appeared in the book.

### "Vassal Capitalists" — Preserved but Theoretically Deepened

The term appears in both texts. In the book it's primarily illustrated through the Apple Store/Google Play examples (third-party developers paying 30% cloud rent) and Amazon vendors. In the 2026 article, the vassal concept receives explicit Marxist framing: "along with the development of the attention economy, the Technostructure empowered Big Business to obtain substantial surplus value produced by smaller businesses elsewhere in the economy." The vassal relationship is now explicitly framed as a *mechanism for redistributing surplus value* — capitalists forced to yield a portion of their surplus value to cloudalists via cloud rent. 

This is the vocabulary of *surplus transfer* being applied to the vassal relationship, which makes it more precise than the book's feudal metaphor. The book's version of "vassal" was primarily illustrative (like a feudal tenant paying a portion of his crop). The 2026 article's version involves a specific claim about surplus value: that cloud rent is a portion of surplus value produced by productive labor, redirected to the cloudalists. This is a genuine conceptual sharpening, not just terminological persistence.

### "The Technostructure" — Genealogical Continuity, Diminished Role

Galbraith's "technostructure" appears in both texts as a key precursor concept. In the book, it gets three chapters of historical analysis (Chapter 2) and serves as the historical bridge between post-war capitalism and the emergence of cloud capital. In the 2026 article, it appears primarily in Section 2 and endnote 3, now positioned more explicitly as capitalism's "monopoly phase" — connecting to Baran and Sweezy's "monopoly surplus" rather than being treated as an independently important analytical category.

The technostructure concept has narrowed in function: in the book it was both an analytical category *and* a historical narrative device; in the 2026 article it's primarily a historical antecedent that demonstrates "rents are endemic to capitalism" (the title of Section 2). Its role in the argument has been *instrumentalized* — it now serves the specific function of establishing that rent proliferation doesn't require the technofeudal hypothesis to explain, thereby isolating what *does* require the hypothesis (the PMBM distinction).

### "The Global Minotaur" — Presence in Book, Near Absence in Article

This is one of the most striking vocabulary shifts. The "Global Minotaur" receives substantial treatment in the book (Chapter 2, the entire account of post-1971 financialization), and connects directly to Varoufakis's prior work of the same name. It's doing significant causal work: the Minotaur's death in 2008 is what created the conditions for cloud capital's rise via central bank money.

In the 2026 article, the Global Minotaur barely appears. It's implied in the discussion of the Nixon Shock and financialization, but the mythological framing is entirely absent. This is significant: the Minotaur was always the most explicitly "mythological" element of Varoufakis's analytical apparatus, and in the 2026 article — which is explicitly responding to Morozov's charge of "Victorian melodrama disguised as social theory" — the mythological vocabulary is quietly retired.

This looks like a *vocabulary death under critical pressure*. Varoufakis doesn't explicitly abandon the Minotaur framework, but he doesn't deploy it in the text that's most explicitly designed to defend the technofeudal hypothesis to a Marxist audience. The absence is telling: the mythological framing was always the most rhetorically powerful but theoretically weakest element, and its disappearance in the more formally rigorous text confirms that it was doing primarily rhetorical work.

### "Surplus Recycling" — Present in Book, Transformed in Article

The "surplus recycling" concept (central to *The Global Minotaur* and present throughout the book's historical account) also diminishes significantly in the 2026 article. In the book, the surplus recycling mechanism is central to understanding the post-1971 global order: profits from Germany and Japan and China get recycled to Wall Street, which finances the Global Minotaur. In the 2026 article, this history is referenced but compressed into brief mentions of the Nixon Shock and financialization, without the surplus recycling vocabulary.

This represents a *partial retirement* of a previously central term. The concept hasn't been rejected — the New Cold War analysis in both texts depends on understanding the recycling mechanism — but the explicit "surplus recycling" vocabulary and its mythological vehicle (the Minotaur) have been demoted from central analytical categories to background historical context.

### New Vocabulary in the 2026 Article: "Techlordism," "HumAIn," "ASRC"

The 2026 article introduces several terms that don't appear in the 2023 book:

**"Techlordism"** — described as "a mutation of transhumanism" that "replaces Homo Economicus at the heart of neoliberalism with a human-AI continuum." This is a new ideological category positioned as the successor to neoliberalism in the same way that Varoufakis argues neoliberalism served as the ideological cover for financialization. The book doesn't have an equivalent term, though it discusses Big Tech's ideological effects.

**"HumAIn"** — referenced as a term used in a 2025 Varoufakis article, denoting the human-AI continuum at the heart of techlordist ideology. This is an extremely recent coinage, post-dating the book by two years.

**"ASRC" (artificial social relations of consumption)** — introduced in Section 8 as a parallel to "social relations of production," designed to extend Marx's dialectical framework to cover the consumption sphere that cloud capital now dominates. This is genuinely new analytical vocabulary that doesn't appear in the book. It's attempting to formalize the book's observation that cloud capital "dominates the social relations of leisure and consumption" (Section 9 of the article).

**"Cloud finance"** — used in both texts but receives much more systematic treatment in the 2026 article, particularly in the discussion of how cloud capital merges with traditional finance (endnote 35, Section 8). In the book it appears primarily in the Chinese context (WeChat payments, digital yuan). In the 2026 article it's positioned as a general phenomenon with implications for the stablecoin discussion.

These new terms mark vocabulary **births** that the 2026 article introduces but doesn't fully develop — they point toward future work rather than consolidating past work. "Techlordism" and "ASRC" are particularly significant because they suggest the next phase of the theoretical elaboration: a theory of how cloud capital shapes ideology and consumption relations, not just production relations.

## II. Concept Trajectories: How Individual Concepts Have Evolved

### "Commodity" — The Pivotal Contested Concept

This is the concept that the prior analysis identified as "the single point of failure" — the commodity boundary between PMCP and PMBM. Tracing it across both texts is essential.

In the book, the commodity distinction is introduced through the father-son narrative and the dual-nature of labor/capital/value framework. The key formulation is early: "you have exited capitalism when you enter amazon.com." The concept then does work throughout Chapter 5 to distinguish cloud rent from profit. But the formal Marxist definition of commodity is not cited explicitly in the main text; it's relegated to Appendix 1 where it appears as a definition ("Commodities are goods or services produced exclusively to be sold").

In the 2026 article, the commodity concept moves from definitional background to *central contested terrain*. Section 4 opens with the direct Marx citation from Capital Vol. 1, Ch. 1: "In order to produce [commodities], he must not only produce use-values, but use-values for others…" The article then states explicitly: "Google's search results, and most of Big Tech's significant products, are not commodities." This is the formal assertion that was implied but not stated with this directness in the book.

The concept has undergone **trajectory: expansion and formalization**. In the book, the commodity distinction is implicit and serves narrative purposes; in the 2026 article it becomes the explicit load-bearing concept that the entire PMBM/PMCP distinction depends on. The Morozov challenge (that Google search results could be treated as commodities produced by PMCP, making Big Tech a "regular capitalist firm") is now directly engaged rather than left as an implicit objection.

What's particularly interesting is the *concession structure* in Section 10: "Today, this separation is even harder to make given the dual nature of the digital technologies involved." This is a direct acknowledgment that the commodity boundary — which the entire argument depends on — is empirically difficult to locate. The book doesn't make this concession explicitly; the 2026 article makes it but immediately deflects it by pointing to the Ricardo precedent (profits and rents were also hard to separate empirically, but the distinction still mattered).

### "Profit vs. Rent" — The Central Structural Opposition

Both texts build on this opposition as their primary analytical distinction. In the book it's framed narratively through the history from feudal ground rent → capitalist profit → cloud rent. In the 2026 article it's framed through the Marxist tradition (Ricardo's warning about ground rent, Baran-Sweezy's monopoly surplus, Harvey's dispossession thesis).

The concept has undergone **narrowing** — in the book, the profit/rent distinction serves multiple purposes (historical, political, analytical); in the 2026 article it primarily serves to locate cloud capital in relation to the surplus value framework. The book's version is broader and more culturally embedded; the article's version is tighter and more technically precise.

One important trajectory: in the book, the claim is made that "capitalism is withering" because profit has been replaced by rent as the driving force. In the 2026 article, this claim is more carefully qualified. Section 10 introduces the language of "parasite" and "host" — cloud capital is a "parasite" living off the "productive department" which still operates on capitalist principles. This is not a rejection of the book's claim but a structural refinement: the book implied that capitalism was dying wholesale; the article models it as two departments (technofeudal and productive) in a dynamic relationship where the parasite could potentially kill the host.

### "The Falling Rate of Profit" — From Implied Background to Formal Result

In the book, the falling rate of profit is mentioned in Appendix 1 (Section 5.1, "Falling rate of profit") as one of capitalism's crisis mechanisms. It's part of the theoretical architecture but not a central rhetorical or analytical emphasis.

In the 2026 article, the falling rate of profit is formalized and extended into the central technical result. Section 7 builds the four-phase model specifically to show that cloud capital *amplifies* the falling profit rate and adds a new tendency: the falling rate of cloud rent extraction. The article explicitly states: "not only is this analysis consistent with Marx's original hypothesis that the rate of profit has a tendency to fall but points to a secular decline in the cloud rent extraction rate as well."

This represents **concept expansion** — from a background mechanism in Appendix 1 to a formal result of the main analysis. The doom-loop (collapsing profits *and* cloud rents) is now a formal prediction rather than a historical observation.

What's interesting about this trajectory is that it makes the argument *more* pessimistic than the book already was. The book's doom-loop was primarily about the relationship between central bank money printing and cloud capital accumulation (printing → cloud capital → more printing → more cloud capital → stagnation). The 2026 article's doom-loop is about the internal dynamics of the system itself: cloud rent extraction reduces the surplus value available for productive investment, which reduces the productive base that cloud rents can be extracted from, which reduces cloud rents, which reduces the stimulus for cloud capital accumulation, which reduces the productive department's squeeze, which allows productive accumulation to recover temporarily — until cloud rents start rising again. This is genuinely a different formulation: it's endogenous rather than relying on central bank behavior.

### "Social Democracy is Impossible" — From Assertion to Structural Derivation

The book asserts the impossibility of social democracy in Chapter 7, primarily through the argument that cloud capital is immune to the regulatory tools that worked against industrial capital (antitrust, price regulation, etc.). The argument is partly historical (social democrats sold out to finance in the 1970s) and partly structural (cloud capital can't be broken up like Standard Oil).

The 2026 article doesn't retract this claim but doesn't foreground it either. In Section 9, the argument pivots to the *positive* case for socialist transformation: "the socialisation of the means of production, distribution, exchange and communication — i.e., society's capital stock, both conventional and cloudalist." The impossibility-of-social-democracy frame recedes in favor of the case for socialization.

This is a subtle **framing shift**: the book's negative formulation ("social democracy is impossible") has been partially replaced by the article's positive formulation ("only socialist transformation can address this"). The political conclusion is similar but the rhetorical trajectory is different — from closing off a familiar option to opening toward an alternative.

### "The Liberal Individual is Dead" — Preserved in Book, Absent in Article

Chapter 7 of the book has an extended section on "The death of the liberal individual" — the argument that cloud capital has eliminated the bourgeois individual's ability to fence off a private sphere from market demands. This is presented through the biographical contrast between the father (who could ring-fence his personal life from the steel plant) and young people today (who must curate their identity continuously online).

In the 2026 article, this concept essentially disappears. The liberal individual death argument is not mentioned. This is striking because it was doing significant normative work in the book — the death of the liberal individual was part of the case for why technofeudalism is *worse* than capitalism, not just different. The prior analysis identified this as part of the "liberal-democratic normative standard" that the book uses but doesn't fully acknowledge.

The absence from the 2026 article suggests this is recognized as peripheral to the *Marxist* case — indeed, from a Marxist perspective, the liberal individual was always a mystification, so its death under cloud capital would be unremarkable. By writing for a Marxist audience, Varoufakis drops a normative claim that would be alien to that audience, which inadvertently confirms that the claim was doing rhetorical rather than analytical work.

### "Cloud Mobilization" — Present in Both, But Functionally Different

In the book's Chapter 7, cloud mobilization is presented as a political strategy: coordinated consumer boycotts + worker strikes targeting cloudalist companies one at a time. The appeal to cloud serfs to withhold their labor/attention is central.

In the 2026 article, the political prescription section (Section 9) is much shorter and differently structured. The emphasis is on the *coalition* question — traditional proletarians, cloud proles, cloud serfs, vassal capitalists — and on the case for socialization of cloud capital. "Cloud mobilization" as a specific tactical concept is not used.

What appears instead is a reference to "Another Now" (the 2020 novel) for the detailed blueprint, and to the Marxist debate about alliances (Lenin's worker-peasant alliance, Mao's popular front including the national bourgeoisie). The 2026 article positions the coalition question as a continuation of classical Marxist debate rather than a novel tactical innovation.

This represents **concept retreat** — cloud mobilization was a specific tactical innovation in the book that now gets folded back into the general question of revolutionary alliances, losing its specific character. The minimal-sacrifice/maximal-gain calculus that the book introduced as cloud mobilization's distinctive advantage is also not mentioned in the 2026 article.

## III. Methodological Shifts: How the Argument is Made

### From Biographical Scaffolding to Formal Model

The most dramatic methodological shift is the disappearance of the biographical scaffold. The entire book is structured as a letter to Varoufakis's deceased father — every chapter opens with address to "Dad," the fireside metallurgy memories recur as metaphors, and the father's 1993 question about the internet anchors the narrative. The 2026 article retains zero of this. It opens with "When my book Technofeudalism was published" — completely impersonal, journal-article tone.

This is a methodological shift from *performative dialectics* (the argument is made by a son explaining things to a father, which enacts the claim about historical materialism being transmitted through generations) to *academic argumentation* (the argument is made through citations, formal models, and direct engagement with named critics).

What's interesting is how this methodological shift affects what can be said. The biographical scaffold allowed Varoufakis to introduce subjective experience as evidence (the feeling of alienation from cloud capital, the contrast between father's ring-fenced life and young people's perpetual curation). The formal academic mode doesn't allow this — it requires either formal proof or citation. Some claims that were doing work in the book (the death of the liberal individual, the emotional register of serfdom) simply can't be made in the article's methodological frame.

### From Mythological Analogy to Marxist Citation

In the book, the primary analytical tools are mythological analogies: the Minotaur, Hesiod's lament, Prometheus, the Movatar. These do real analytical work — they provide causal narratives that are both emotionally vivid and structurally precise (the Minotaur as a recycling mechanism, for instance).

In the 2026 article, the primary analytical tools are Marx citations and formal models. Section 4 alone contains five direct Marx citations (Capital Vol. 1, Ch. 1; Capital Vol. 1, Ch. 33; Theories of Surplus Value; the Grundrisse; Capital Vol. 3, Ch. 50). The mythological vocabulary is entirely absent.

This is the methodological equivalent of the vocabulary shift from Minotaur to formal model. The analytical work is being done by a different apparatus. What's lost is the cultural breadth — the ability to connect economic analysis to Homer, Sophocles, and Hesiod was part of what made the book's argument resonate beyond specialist audiences. What's gained is analytical precision and defensibility against Marxist critics who would be unmoved by mythological analogies.

### From Keynesian Aggregate Demand to Marxist Surplus Value

This is perhaps the most significant methodological shift, and it's not fully acknowledged in the 2026 article. The book's analysis of the 2008 crisis and its aftermath is fundamentally Keynesian in structure: the problem is insufficient aggregate demand, austerity deepens the demand shortfall, central bank money fails to revive investment because investors see no demand for their products, etc. The Keynesian framework is explicit in Chapter 4: "What we spend collectively translates automatically into what we earn collectively."

The 2026 article retains this background but formally foregrounds the Marxist falling-rate-of-profit analysis instead. The formal model in Section 7 is built on profit rates and organic composition of capital — pure Marx, no Keynes. Aggregate demand appears only briefly and without formal treatment.

This shift matters because the Keynesian and Marxist frameworks generate different predictions and different political conclusions. The Keynesian diagnosis (demand deficiency → stagnation) is compatible with social democratic remedies (fiscal stimulus, redistribution). The Marxist diagnosis (falling profit rate → structural crisis) is not. By formally prioritizing the Marxist framework in the 2026 article, Varoufakis is strengthening the case against social democracy at the theoretical level, even as he softens the political tone compared to the book.

## IV. Metaphor Evolution: What Images Are Used and Dropped

### The Parasite/Host Metaphor — New and Structurally Important

The book doesn't use the parasite/host metaphor explicitly. It uses the feudal overlord/vassal/serf metaphor extensively. The 2026 article introduces "parasite/host" as its central structural metaphor: "the technofeudal expansion is checked and the productive department reclaims part of its lost ground… Phase 3 is a period during which the technofeudal expansion is checked and the productive department suffers the fate of a parasite that sucked too much energy and nurture from its host." (Section 7, Phase 3 description)

And in Section 10: "Has a new ruling class (the cloudalists or techlords) emerged as dominant on the coattails of cloud capital? Are their cloud rents macroeconomically inconsequential bubbles on a torrent of capitalist profits? Or are capitalist profits increasingly turning into frothy bubbles on a vortex of cloud rents?"

The parasite/host metaphor is not merely decorative — it's doing structural work. It models the relationship between the technofeudal and productive departments as a *predator-prey dynamic*, explicitly borrowing from Goodwin (1967)'s Lotka-Volterra economic model. This gives the metaphor formal grounding: the parasite/host relationship generates specific predictions (Phase 1 through Phase 4 dynamics, ultimate collapse of both profit rates and cloud rent extraction rates).

This represents a **metaphor birth** that is also a *methodological advance* — the ecological metaphor is connected to a formal model in a way that the feudal metaphors never were. The book's feudal imagery was evocative but not formally tractable; the 2026 article's ecological imagery is directly connected to differential equations.

### The Feudal Metaphor — Retreat but Not Abandonment

The feudal vocabulary (serfs, overlords, fiefs, vassals) appears in both texts but with different density. In the book, these terms are consistently capitalized (Cloud Serfs, Cloud Fiefs, Vassal Capitalists) and form a coherent alternative vocabulary that *replaces* capitalist terminology in the domain of technofeudalism. In the 2026 article, they appear but are more integrated into the formal analytical vocabulary — "cloud fiefs" appears alongside PMBM and PMCP without the same rhetorical elevation.

This represents **metaphor demotion** — the feudal vocabulary remains in use but loses its dominant status as the primary organizational framework. The formal Marxist vocabulary (surplus value, organic composition of capital, PMCP vs. PMBM) takes over the organizational role.

One specific change: in the book, "vassals" is applied consistently to small capitalist firms trapped in cloud fiefs. In the 2026 article (Section 9), the word appears in a more explicitly political-historical context: "vassal, small-scale capitalists facing ruin in the shadow of cloud capital" — referencing Georgi Dimitrov's popular front analysis and Mao's "national bourgeoisie." This recontextualizes the vassal category from an economic description to a political alliance question, which is a genuine conceptual shift.

### The Faustian Deal Metaphor — Preserved

Both texts use the "Faustian bargain" or "Faustian deal" metaphor for the user's relationship to cloud capital ("To use the personalised services their algorithms provide, we must submit to a business model based on the harvesting of our data" — book; "the Dark Deal" in the context of US-China relations — book; the "Faustian deal" framing in the 2026 article's Section 8 on AI: "an AI-powered version of Alexa... will gradually cultivate in you a neuro-dependence that makes it indispensable to your psyche"). 

This metaphor has *migrated* across applications. In the book it was primarily about the individual user's relationship to cloud capital. In the 2026 article it's been extended to describe the broader cultural and psychological effect of AI-powered cloud capital on the human-machine relationship. The metaphor has expanded in scope.

## V. Framing Shifts: What Was Tentative, What Is Now Axiomatic

### "Capitalism Is Dead" — From Hypothesis to Established Premise

The book opens with explicit tentativeness: "So, what is my hypothesis? It is that capitalism is now dead." The word "hypothesis" is significant — Varoufakis is flagging that this is a conjecture to be argued for.

The 2026 article's framing is different: "Conceived as a Marxist analysis of the present historical moment... a presentation of its thesis tailored for Marxist readers was always on the cards." The word "thesis" has replaced "hypothesis." By 2026, Varoufakis is no longer presenting this as a conjecture to be tested; it's a thesis to be defended.

This framing shift (hypothesis → thesis) is significant because it changes the epistemic relationship to evidence. A hypothesis invites falsification; a thesis invites defense. The 2026 article is explicitly a defense, not a test.

The 2026 article does acknowledge that the empirical case is harder to make than the theoretical case (Section 10's admission about measurement difficulties). But this acknowledgment is framed as a concession about measurement difficulties, not as evidence of theoretical uncertainty. The thesis is maintained while the evidential bar is raised — but not to the level where it could falsify the thesis.

### "Social Democracy Is Impossible" — From Political Claim to Structural Derivation

In the book this is a political claim argued through historical narrative (social democrats sold out to finance, cloud capital can't be regulated like industrial capital). In the 2026 article the claim is repositioned as a consequence of the formal model: because the technofeudal department necessarily grows at the expense of the productive department (the Phase 1-4 dynamics), and because social democratic intervention works through regulatory pressure on productive capital (antitrust, price regulation), there is no regulatory mechanism that can address the cloud capital problem at its source. The structural argument for the impossibility of social democracy is now stronger because it's derived from the formal model rather than asserted on historical grounds.

### "The New Cold War Is About Cloud Capital" — From Observation to Explanatory Framework

In the book, the New Cold War analysis in Chapter 6 is presented as an application of the technofeudal framework: the framework *explains* the Huawei ban, the TikTok restrictions, Biden's semiconductor embargo. This is an *argumentative* use of the framework — it's being tested against a real phenomenon and shown to have explanatory power.

In the 2026 article (Section 8), the New Cold War is mentioned briefly as an established consequence: "As cloud capital merged with traditional finance into what I call cloud finance, it ignited the New Cold War between the United States and China giving imperialism a new twist." The New Cold War has moved from *explanandum* (thing to be explained) to *explicatum* (thing that confirms the framework). This is a framing shift from tentative application to confirmed result.

## VI. Intellectual Interlocutors: Who Is Now Referenced

The 2023 book references Galbraith, Marx, Keynes, Smith, Ricardo, and various historical figures (Edison, Ford, etc.). It mentions Shoshana Zuboff and Cédric Durand in the "Influences" section as sympathetic predecessors whose work Varoufakis extends, and McKenzie Wark as a kindred spirit with a different conceptual vocabulary.

The 2026 article's reference list is dramatically expanded and differently oriented: Baran and Sweezy (monopoly capital theory), Harvey (dispossession), Hilferding (finance capital), Luxemburg (accumulation), Lenin (imperialism), Mirowski (neoliberalism), Goodwin (growth cycle), Wood (origin of capitalism), Foley (rethinking financial capitalism). These are all Marxist or post-Keynesian political economists. The intellectual interlocutors have shifted from the public intellectual sphere to the Marxist academy.

Also notable: Morozov (2022) and (2025) are explicitly engaged, as are Snow (2023), Addison and Eisenberg (2025). The 2026 article is operating in a *polemical* mode that the book didn't employ — it's responding to critics by name, which reflects a different stage in the argument's lifecycle (defense rather than initial presentation).

This shift in intellectual interlocutors affects what counts as a good argument. In the book, a good argument convinces a general educated reader. In the 2026 article, a good argument engages with the specific technical objections raised by Marxist political economists (Foley's point about productive vs. unproductive labor, Morozov's point about search results as commodities, Snow's point about formal freedom to quit platforms).

## VII. The Most Surprising Discoveries

**The ASRC coinage** (artificial social relations of consumption) is perhaps the most genuinely new conceptual move in the 2026 article. It extends the Marxist framework — which analyzes contradictions between social relations of *production* and produced means of *production* — to the consumption sphere by introducing social relations of *consumption* as a new dialectical term. This is not elaborated at length in the article, but it's pointed at explicitly as the framework for understanding AI's impact on cloud capital. It's the seed of a third generation of the argument.

**The retreat of the biographical father figure** is analytically significant in a way that goes beyond methodological difference. The father served a specific structural function in the book: he embodied the historical materialist tradition (literally a communist who studied ancient metallurgy), and his question about the internet provided the book with its narrative organizing principle. The complete absence of this figure from the 2026 article is not just methodological — it removes the implicit claim that Marxist analysis is a *living tradition transmitted between generations*. The 2026 article's mode is that of a scholar defending a thesis before hostile peers, not a son answering a father's question. The shift changes the emotional relationship of the reader to the argument.

**The Doctorow citation** in the 2026 article (endnote 7 and the "enshittification" reference) represents a new intellectual alliance. Cory Doctorow is a tech critic and writer rather than an academic economist, and his concept of "enshittification" (platforms degrade their service to users once they've achieved lock-in, then degrade service to business customers once they've been locked in) is compatible with the cloud fief analysis. Bringing Doctorow in suggests Varoufakis is building bridges to the digital rights / tech criticism community as well as the Marxist academic community.

**The Assange private communication** in endnote 32 is remarkable. It cites a private communication from Julian Assange on the nature of AI and surveillance, connecting the technofeudal analysis to WikiLeaks-style transparency politics. This is not in the book at all. It suggests that by 2026, Varoufakis is locating his analysis within a broader political-intellectual world that includes the radical transparency movement — an alliance with implications for the political strategy dimension of the argument.

**The DeepSeek discussion** (Section 8) is entirely absent from the 2023 book (as it couldn't have been). But it's analytically significant because it confirms a specific prediction: if cloud capital's value comes from behavior modification rather than commodity production, then the moment a cheaper alternative commodity supplier (DeepSeek vs. ChatGPT) appears, the incumbents will shift to the behavior modification / cloud rent model. Varoufakis presents this as a vindication of the PMBM analysis: "Any company that, today, continues to try to profit by supplying AI-based commodities will either have to switch to extracting cloud rents in the technofeudal department or perish." This is a genuine empirical test being conducted in real time, which is unusual for a theoretical argument.

## VIII. Summary of Key Evolutionary Patterns

Across the vocabulary and concept dimensions:

1. **Formalization over dramatization**: The feudal metaphors and biographical scaffolding do less work; formal Marxist vocabulary (PMCP, PMBM, organic composition of cloud capital) does more. The argument is becoming more technical and less culturally accessible.

2. **The PMBM/PMCP distinction births and becomes load-bearing**: This is the single most important conceptual development from book to article.

3. **The commodity boundary becomes an explicit contested terrain rather than an implicit one**: This represents maturation of the argument — the book's implicit commodity exclusion claim is now explicitly defended against Morozov's challenge.

4. **Mythology retreats under critical pressure**: The Minotaur, surplus recycling, and the father-letter format all disappear or diminish significantly in the article written explicitly for Marxist readers who would be unmoved by mythological framing.

5. **The falling rate of profit becomes the formal unifying result**: Rather than being a background mechanism, it becomes the core technical conclusion of the four-phase model.

6. **Political strategy retreats from specific tactics to general alliance question**: Cloud mobilization gives way to the classical Marxist debate about revolutionary alliances.

7. **"Hypothesis" becomes "thesis" becomes "established premise"**: The epistemic status of the technofeudal claim shifts across texts.

8. **New vocabulary points to next generation of argument**: ASRC, techlordism, HumAIn, cloud finance as a general concept — these suggest the theoretical elaboration is ongoing and will extend into ideology, AI, and monetary system analysis.

The analytical emphasis specified calls for attention to Varoufakis's coined vocabulary (surplus recycling, Minotaur mechanism, technofeudalism, democratic deficit), his methodological signature (Keynesian aggregate demand + mythological framing), Morozov's contested claims, and the inferential commitments embedded in the economic diagnosis. The prior analysis has substantially mapped these. What this pass adds is the **inferential architecture of the dimensional comparisons** — tracing what follows necessarily from vocabulary, methodology, metaphor, and framing changes, and showing where Varoufakis's own argument commits him to positions he doesn't fully embrace.

## I. Vocabulary Evolution: Semantic Drift, Birth, Death, and Mutation

### A. Terms That Persist with Semantic Drift

**"Technofeudalism"** — Both texts deploy this term, but its semantic content has shifted in a precise and diagnostic way. In the 2023 book, "technofeudalism" functions primarily as a *polemical neologism*: it is announced, defended against objections, and then deployed as an organizing vocabulary for everything from Musk's Twitter purchase to the New Cold War. The book's Chapter 5 ("What's in a Word?") is explicitly a defense of the term's political utility against alternatives like "platform capitalism" or "hyper-capitalism." The term there is *aspirational-diagnostic* — it names a transformation that the author claims is *occurring*, and the naming is itself a political act, following Simone Weil's dictum that defining words precisely can save lives.

In the 2026 article, "technofeudalism" is semantically repositioned as a *theoretical hypothesis* to be defended before a Marxist audience. The article opens: "a presentation of its thesis tailored for Marxist readers was always on the cards." The word now carries a different epistemic load — it must satisfy formal criteria (consistency with Marx's commodity theory, the falling rate of profit, unproductive labor) rather than rhetorical criteria (emotional resonance, political mobilization). This is a **semantic drift from political vocabulary to theoretical category**. The shift is not a retraction but a hardening: the term now enters a formal apparatus and must earn its place there.

The inferential consequence of this drift is significant: in the book, objecting to "technofeudalism" is objecting to a political intervention; in the article, objecting requires engaging with formal Marxist categories (commodity, productive labor, organic composition of cloud capital). Morozov's critique — that the term imports "Victorian melodrama" — targets precisely the book's political register, which the article's register is designed to make irrelevant. The drift is thus partly a strategic response to Morozov.

**"Cloud capital"** — The term persists across both texts but undergoes **definitional hardening** and terminological supplementation. In the book, "cloud capital" is introduced as a *produced means of behavioral modification* (that exact phrase appears in Appendix 1), but the formal apparatus is relegated to the Appendix while the main text uses narrative illustration (the Alexa dialogue, the amazon.com science fiction scene). In the 2026 article, "cloud capital" is *formally defined* as PMBM (produced means of behavioral modification) and contrasted explicitly with PMCP (produced means of commodity production) — a distinction that was implicit in the book but never carried its own acronym.

The semantic drift here is from *illustrated concept* to *formal analytical category*. PMCP/PMBM is genuinely new vocabulary that the book doesn't contain. What this means inferentially: in the book, the non-commodity status of Google search results is asserted by narrative; in the article, it's derived from Marx's definition of commodity (Capital Vol. 1, Ch. 1, cited directly). If Morozov accepts the Marx citation, he must engage the PMCP/PMBM distinction rather than simply denying that cloud capital is qualitatively different.

**"Rent" / "Cloud rent"** — Both texts use "cloud rent" extensively, but the 2026 article introduces a key concession not present in the book: the acknowledgment that *measuring* cloud rent is as difficult as measuring Ricardo's ground rent or Baran and Sweezy's monopoly surplus. Endnote 7 of the article explicitly states: "the accounting profession proved neither interested nor capable of accounting for these separately." The book presents cloud rent as observable and analytically clear; the article admits its empirical opacity while defending the conceptual distinction.

This represents a **semantic supplementation of a prior term**: the concept is unchanged but now comes equipped with an explicit epistemological defense of why the concept matters even when it cannot be directly measured. The inferential commitment embedded in this move: the unmeasurability of cloud rent cannot be used to dismiss it, any more than Ricardo's inability to measure ground rent separately from profit means the distinction doesn't exist. But this defense simultaneously creates a **self-sealing structure**: the argument cannot be falsified by pointing to the absence of a cloud rent line item in accounting statements.

**"Cloud serfs" / "Cloud proles"** — Terminologically stable across both texts, but the *mechanism* of serfdom has been deepened. The book explains cloud serfdom through the observation that users perform unpaid labor that enriches cloudalists. The 2026 article (endnote 29) adds the crucial switching-cost mechanism: "with every one of our posts our costs of switching away from Instagram increase. Thus, without doing anything, Meta sees its cloud capital... accumulate in direct proportion to the unwaged labour of its users which, inadvertently increases their switching costs." 

This is not merely elaboration — it is a **conceptual enrichment that changes the mechanism**. In the book, cloud serfdom is extraction of unpaid labor *simpliciter*. In the article, cloud serfdom is a **self-reinforcing trap**: the labor doesn't just enrich the cloudalist, it raises the cost of the serf's own exit. This changes the political prescription logic: if the mechanism is switching costs rather than mere extraction, then the appropriate response is interoperability requirements (reducing switching costs) as much as labor organizing. But Varoufakis never endorses interoperability requirements as sufficient — he dismisses regulation generally — which creates an internal tension the article doesn't resolve.

### B. Terms Born in the 2026 Article

**PMCP / PMBM** — These acronyms are genuinely new. They formalize the distinction between productive and cloud capital in a way that the book's vocabulary couldn't. The PMCP/PMBM distinction is the single most important vocabulary birth in the 2026 article. As the Discovery Pass noted, everything in the technofeudal hypothesis depends on this distinction holding: if Google search results can be treated as PMCP-produced commodities, the entire apparatus collapses back into digital monopoly capitalism. 

The inferential commitment here is deep: Varoufakis must show not merely that Big Tech *describes* its services as free gifts rather than commodities, but that the services *are* — by Marx's definition — not commodities. His argument is: commodities are produced to be exchanged through a market; Google search results are produced to modify behavior and are not exchanged; therefore they are not commodities. Morozov's counter is that this analysis is question-begging: Google search results could be described as commodities produced by machines (PMCP), given away at zero price as a loss-leader to finance advertising revenue. The PMCP/PMBM distinction does not resolve this dispute — it names it more precisely.

**ASRC (artificial social relations of consumption)** — Introduced in Section 8 as the consumption-sphere parallel to "social relations of production." This is pointed at rather than developed: it suggests a future theoretical extension where the dialectical contradiction between ASRC and PMBM becomes the next-generation analytical engine. The term points toward post-human theory — the moment when the contradiction between what we feel toward AI systems (ASRC) and what those systems actually do (PMBM) becomes historically generative. This is vocabulary birth that anticipates the third phase of the argument.

**"Techlordism" / "HumAIn"** — Both are ideological-category neologisms absent from the book. "Techlordism" is positioned as the successor ideology to neoliberalism in the way neoliberalism was the ideological cover for financialization. "HumAIn" (human-AI continuum) names the anthropological premise of that ideology. These terms mark an extension of the argument into the realm of ideology that the book addresses (Chapter 7's "death of the liberal individual") but doesn't name as systematically.

### C. Terms That Die or Diminish

**"The Global Minotaur"** — Nearly absent from the 2026 article. The Minotaur was central to the book (an entire chapter, recurrent throughout), serving as the mythological vehicle for the recycling mechanism analysis. In the article, the mechanism is present (Nixon Shock, financialization, the post-2008 period) but the Minotaur vocabulary is gone. This is **vocabulary death under critical pressure**: writing for a Marxist audience that Morozov has accused of deploying "Victorian melodrama disguised as social theory," the mythological vehicle becomes a liability. The same causal analysis is performed by different vocabulary (references to Hilferding, Luxemburg, Lenin).

**"Surplus recycling"** — Similarly diminished. The concept survives in substance (the Dark Deal chapter analysis is still present in the 2026 article's geopolitical section), but the phrase "surplus recycling" with its Global Minotaur framing is absent. What replaces it analytically is the two-sector productive/technofeudal dynamic model, which generates recycling-like predictions through differential equations rather than mythological narrative. This represents **metaphor-to-model translation**: the intuition that was carried by the Minotaur metaphor is now formalized, losing its cultural reach but gaining analytical precision.

**"Liberal individual"** — The "death of the liberal individual" section from Chapter 7 of the book is entirely absent from the 2026 article. As the Discovery Pass noted, this was doing normative work in the book (demonstrating that technofeudalism is *worse* than capitalism by liberal-democratic standards) that would be alien to a Marxist audience for whom the liberal individual was always already a mystification. The absence confirms the prior analysis's finding: the liberal-democratic normative standard was doing rhetorical work in the book that cannot translate to the article's target audience.

## II. Methodology Evolution: How the Argument Is Made

### A. The Disappearance of the Biographical Scaffold

This is the most structurally significant methodological shift, and it warrants inference beyond what the Discovery Pass established. The biographical scaffold (letter to the deceased father) was not merely a stylistic choice — it was performing specific analytical work:

1. It authorized subjective experience as evidence. The father's question ("will the internet prove capitalism's undoing or render it invincible?") gave the entire argument its narrative engine. The father's death framed the argument as a memorial obligation — politically urgent but also morally charged.

2. It enacted historical materialism as a *living tradition*. The father introduced Varoufakis to metallurgy, which introduced him to historical materialism, which led to the analysis of cloud capital. The argument's genealogy was also its intellectual pedigree.

3. It allowed the biographical frame to bear claims that formal academic prose cannot. "I envy the way you lived, Dad. You were the epitome of the liberal individual" is a claim about what capitalism afforded in an earlier era — but it's a claim made through nostalgia rather than evidence, which insulates it from empirical challenge.

The 2026 article's elimination of this scaffold has inferential consequences that the Discovery Pass didn't fully trace. Without biographical authorization, every claim must survive on its formal merits. This means that the assertions most dependent on the biographical frame — the death of the liberal individual, the emotional register of "mind-chains," the claim that cloud serfs "no longer own their minds" — cannot be made in the article's mode. The article is therefore not just a different *presentation* of the same argument but a **selective version** of it: the claims that survive the move from biography to formalism are those that can be grounded in Marxist category theory. The claims that don't survive reveal what the argument was actually doing in the book that formal analysis cannot replicate.

### B. The Shift from Keynesian Aggregate Demand to Marxist Surplus Value

This methodological shift is deeper than the Discovery Pass acknowledged. The book's analytical backbone, while containing Marxist vocabulary, is fundamentally **Keynesian in structure**: the crisis of 2008 is primarily a demand deficiency crisis; central bank money printing fails because austerity eliminates customer income; cloud capital is funded by interest-free central bank money because capitalists won't invest in an economy without demand. The aggregate demand logic — "what we spend collectively translates automatically into what we earn collectively" — is the core explanatory mechanism.

The 2026 article's Section 7 replaces this with the **falling rate of profit analysis**: cloud capital amplifies the tendency for the profit rate to fall by increasing the organic composition of cloud capital. This is formally Marxist rather than Keynesian. The four-phase model (Sections 7 and endnotes 23-29) is built on differential equations modeled on Goodwin (1967), which is a Marxist growth-cycle model, not a Keynesian demand-deficiency model.

The inferential consequence of this methodological shift is significant: the Keynesian explanation for cloud capital's rise (central bank money created the conditions) is *compatible with* social democratic solutions (better fiscal policy, more progressive taxation, regulation of financial markets). The falling-rate-of-profit explanation is *not compatible* with social democratic solutions — it suggests that cloud capital's dominance is the result of internal capitalist dynamics, not of contingent policy choices. By formally prioritizing the Marxist framework in the 2026 article, Varoufakis strengthens the case against reform *as a theoretical matter*, not merely as a political one.

However, the Keynesian explanation doesn't simply disappear from the article — it's compressed into Section 8's references to the Nixon Shock and financialization. What we have is **methodological stratification**: the Marxist falling-rate analysis occupies the formal foreground while the Keynesian story remains as historical background. The question of which is doing the actual explanatory work — which is why cloud capital dominates *now* rather than having dominated earlier — is left undertheorized.

### C. The Shift from Named Evidence to Formal Citation

The book uses named individuals as evidence: Juan Espinoza (Amazon warehouse worker), Thomas Peel (historical capitalist), the London Stock Exchange spike on August 12, 2020. The 2026 article's evidence is primarily citation-based: Baran and Sweezy (1966), Goodwin (1967), Foley (2013), Morozov (2022, 2025), Snow (2023). The shift from named individuals to named theorists represents the methodological difference between public intellectual argument and academic political economy.

This matters inferentially because of what each evidential mode can and cannot do. Named individuals provide phenomenological evidence — the experience of Amazon warehouse workers establishes that cloud proles exist and suffer. Academic citations provide theoretical authority — Foley's analysis establishes what "productive labor" means in the Marxist tradition. Neither mode can do the other's work. The book needed the phenomenological evidence to make its case legible to a general audience; the article needs theoretical citations to make its case credible to Marxist scholars. The loss of phenomenological evidence in the article means the argument's human stakes are invisible unless the reader already knows them from the book.

## III. Metaphor Evolution: What Images Structure Thought

### A. The Parasite/Host Metaphor — A New Structural Metaphor

The Discovery Pass correctly identified the parasite/host metaphor as new and structurally important. What remains to be traced is the inferential architecture this metaphor creates.

The 2026 article's Phase 3 is described as "the fate of a parasite that sucked too much energy and nurture from its host." This is not decorative. The parasite/host metaphor structures a **specific prediction**: that the technofeudal department is self-limiting, that it will collapse when it has extracted too much from the productive department that sustains it. This prediction is structurally important because it suggests that the system has a built-in terminus: technofeudalism is not stable, it is self-undermining.

The feudal metaphors in the book didn't generate this prediction. Cloud fiefs, overlords, and vassals evoke a *stable* power structure (feudalism lasted centuries). The parasite/host metaphor, by contrast, built into the system's dynamics a collapse mechanism. This represents a **metaphor change that changes the prediction**: from "technofeudalism will consolidate and stabilize" (feudal metaphor logic) to "technofeudalism will intensify and then collapse" (parasite/host metaphor logic). The political implications differ: the former suggests urgent intervention before consolidation; the latter suggests preparing for the inevitable collapse.

The inferential commitment embedded in this metaphor shift: if technofeudalism is parasitic and self-undermining, then the political strategy of waiting for the collapse and preparing alternatives becomes more coherent than cloud mobilization before the collapse. Varoufakis doesn't draw this implication, presumably because the book's political urgency depends on the threat being *present and growing*, not *present but self-limiting*. The metaphor and the political prescription are in tension.

### B. The Feudal Metaphor — Demotion, Not Death

The feudal vocabulary (serfs, fiefs, vassals, overlords) persists in both texts but at different rhetorical registers. In the book, the feudal metaphors are *constitutive* — the argument cannot be understood without them because they provide the organizing categories. In the 2026 article, the feudal metaphors appear *illustratively* — they name things that are formally defined by Marxist categories (PMCP, PMBM, unproductive labor).

The inferential consequence: in the book, accepting "cloud serfs" requires accepting the emotional load of the medieval vocabulary (exploitation, bondage, degradation). In the article, accepting cloud serfs requires accepting Marx's definition of unproductive labor. The latter is a narrower, more precise, but also more contestable commitment. Morozov can challenge the application of "unproductive labor" to cloud serf activity without challenging the word "serf" emotionally. The formalization that replaces rhetoric with precision simultaneously replaces rhetorical force with formal vulnerability.

### C. The Star Trek / Hesiod Metaphors — Structural Absence

Neither Hesiod's lament nor the Star Trek "Cloud Minders" episode appears in the 2026 article. These were the book's *frame metaphors* — the images that set the terms for understanding technology's relationship to humanity before any economic analysis begins. Their absence in the article means the argument has no mythological frame, only a theoretical one.

This is methodologically significant: Hesiod's lament established that technology has *always* created both liberation and enslavement, that this dual nature is the human condition. The article replaces this with Brecht's observation that real drama consists of "two things that shouldn't be able to exist at the same time" — capitalism and technofeudalism coexisting. The shift is from *mythological time* (repeating patterns from antiquity to the present) to *dialectical time* (contradictions that generate their own supersession). This is not merely a stylistic difference — it changes what kind of historical understanding the argument claims to provide.

## IV. Framing Evolution: Confidence Trajectories and Epistemic Status

### A. Hypothesis to Thesis to Established Premise

The book opens: "So, what is my hypothesis? It is that capitalism is now dead." The 2026 article opens by announcing a defense of "its thesis." The Discovery Pass noted this shift from hypothesis to thesis, but the fuller picture is more nuanced:

In the book, "hypothesis" is used strategically but inconsistently. It signals epistemic humility for the reader while the argument itself proceeds with rhetorical confidence. Phrases like "capitalism is *now* dead" and "you have *exited* capitalism when you enter amazon.com" are not hedged as hypotheses — they're asserted as observations. The book presents itself as a hypothesis while arguing as if it were a confirmed finding.

The 2026 article is more epistemically honest in one direction and less so in another. It acknowledges the measurement problem directly (Section 10: "this separation is even harder to make given the dual nature of the digital technologies involved"). But it simultaneously shifts the rhetorical framing from "hypothesis" to "thesis" and "analysis," which implies a higher epistemic standing. The formal model (Sections 7, endnotes 23-29) is presented as *proof* of the doom-loop dynamics — but the model's parameters are unspecified from existing data. The model demonstrates that *if* the formal conditions hold, the dynamics follow — but whether the conditions hold is empirically undetermined.

The framing trajectory is therefore: **greater epistemic honesty about measurement + greater rhetorical confidence about theoretical standing = roughly similar overall epistemic status, but different vulnerabilities**. The book was vulnerable to the objection "you haven't proven your hypothesis." The article is vulnerable to the objection "your formal model assumes what it needs to prove — you haven't established the parameters empirically."

### B. The Framing of Social Democracy's Impossibility

The framing of this claim has shifted in both direction and mode. In the book (Chapter 7), the impossibility of social democracy is a **political claim** argued historically: social democrats made a Faustian bargain with finance capital, and cloud capital is immune to the regulatory tools that constrained industrial capital. The framing is narrative and political — it tells a story of betrayal and structural incapacity.

In the 2026 article, the same conclusion is implicit but never foregrounded as such. The structural derivation (Section 7's four-phase model shows that cloud rent extraction necessarily degrades the productive sector) does the same political work but presents it as a **formal result** rather than a political claim. The shift is from "social democracy is impossible because history shows it failed and cloud capital defeats it" to "social democracy is insufficient because the formal dynamics of the technofeudal department cannot be addressed by mechanisms calibrated for productive capital."

The inferential consequence: in the book, one could object that social democracy failed for contingent political reasons and might succeed if sufficiently radicalized. In the article, that objection must engage with the formal model — it must show that the model's dynamics don't hold, or that social democratic mechanisms can address what the model identifies as structural drivers. The framing shift moves the burden of proof.

### C. The New Cold War: From Application to Confirmation

In the book (Chapter 6), the New Cold War analysis applies the technofeudal framework: here is a real phenomenon (Huawei ban, TikTok restrictions, semiconductor embargo), and the framework *explains* it. This is an argumentative use — the framework is being tested against a real-world case and shown to have explanatory power.

In the 2026 article (Section 8), the New Cold War is one of several *consequences* of cloud capital's rise: "As cloud capital merged with traditional finance into what I call cloud finance, it ignited the New Cold War." The framing has shifted from **explanandum to explicatum** — from "here is a thing to be explained" to "here is something the theory predicts." This is the framing shift characteristic of *paradigm consolidation*: phenomena that were once evidence for the theory become deductions from it.

This shift is not warranted by new evidence. The factual content (Huawei ban, digital yuan, DeepSeek disruption) is updated, but the logical relationship between framework and phenomenon has been reversed through framing rather than through argument. The DeepSeek discussion is explicitly presented as a "vindication" — a real-time empirical test that confirms the prediction. Whether DeepSeek actually confirms the PMBM prediction (that AI companies will shift from commodity production to behavioral modification) depends on whether DeepSeek actually caused OpenAI and Google to shift strategies, which is asserted but not demonstrated.

## V. Concept Trajectory: Lifecycles of Individual Concepts

### A. "Commodity" — From Implicit Exclusion to Explicit Contest

The lifecycle of the commodity concept is the most revealing in the entire evolution. In the book, the non-commodity status of Google search results and other Big Tech outputs is asserted as obvious through narrative: "you have exited capitalism when you enter amazon.com." The commodity exclusion is performed rhetorically — the reader is invited to see that amazon.com is *not* a market — but the formal argument for why the outputs are not commodities is confined to Appendix 1.

In the 2026 article, the commodity concept moves from *assumed background* to *central contested terrain*. Section 4 opens with direct Marx citation and then explicitly states: "Google's search results, and most of Big Tech's significant products, are not commodities." This is the formal assertion of what the book merely performed. And then Morozov's challenge is directly engaged: if one accepts that Google is producing search-result commodities using massive capital investment, the technofeudal hypothesis collapses.

The concept has undergone **trajectory: formalization of what was implicit**. But formalization makes the contested point more vulnerable, not less. In the book, the reader who accepts the narrative ("amazon.com is a fief") has already accepted the non-commodity conclusion. In the article, the reader must accept a specific Marxist definition of commodity and then accept its application to digital outputs — and this is precisely where the argument is most exposed.

The inferential commitment that follows: if the commodity definition is the load-bearing concept, and if it's contested by sophisticated Marxist critics (Morozov, Foley), then the entire downstream architecture of the argument is conditional on winning a definitional dispute. Varoufakis cannot win that dispute by asserting it — he can only win it by showing that his application of Marx's definition is more consistent with the overall system of Marx's value theory than Morozov's alternative application. Section 4 attempts this but doesn't fully succeed: it shows that behavioral modification machines are *functionally different* from productive machines, but the commodity definition Varoufakis uses is about the intended exchange relationship, not about functional mode. A capitalist can intend to extract advertising revenue from providing a search result at zero price — in which case the search result is still produced for exchange, just exchange of attention rather than direct payment.

### B. "Profit" vs. "Rent" — Narrowing and Stabilization

This opposition was central in the book (the entire "What's in a Word?" chapter) and remains central in the 2026 article, but has undergone **conceptual narrowing**. In the book, the profit/rent distinction does historical work (narrating the trajectory from feudal ground rent through capitalist profit to cloud rent), political work (establishing that something fundamentally different from capitalism is occurring), and analytical work (distinguishing cloud capital's returns from productive capital's returns). All three functions are blended together.

In the 2026 article, the profit/rent distinction is primarily doing **analytical work**: cloud rent is formally distinguished from profit through the PMCP/PMBM distinction (cloud capital produces no commodities, so its returns cannot be profit in the technical sense). The historical and political work is still present but compressed.

The narrowing is an analytical gain — the distinction is more precise — but a rhetorical loss. The book's reader felt the historical sweep of rent's return; the article's reader must engage with a formal distinction. Whether this trade-off serves the argument's overall goals depends on whether the 2026 article is meant to *build on* the book's political work or to *replace* it for a different audience.

### C. "Cloud Mobilization" — Retreat and Absorption

The concept has undergone **trajectory: absorption into a general framework**. In the book, cloud mobilization is a specific tactical innovation with its own logic (minimal personal sacrifice, maximal collective gain) and a specific scenario (international Amazon boycott coordinated with warehouse strikes). In the 2026 article, this specific tactical concept is entirely absent.

What replaces it is the invocation of classical Marxist debate about revolutionary alliances (Lenin, Trotsky, Mao, Dimitrov) applied to the cloud proles + cloud serfs + vassal capitalists coalition question. The specific innovation of cloud mobilization — its inversion of the collective action calculus — is lost in the translation to the alliance framework. The question becomes "which classes can be organized together" rather than "how does cloud capital enable a new kind of political action."

The inferential consequence: the 2026 article implicitly concedes that cloud mobilization requires something more than tactical innovation — it requires a theory of revolutionary alliance that locates cloud proles and cloud serfs within the broader class structure. But the classical alliance debates (worker-peasant, popular front) were debates about organizing *productive classes* with real material stakes. Inserting cloud serfs (whose exploitation is not recognized as such because it feels like voluntary participation) into these frameworks without modification is precisely the problem that the specific cloud mobilization concept was designed to address. Its absorption into the alliance debate framework may be a conceptual regression.

## VII. Key Inferential Findings from Dimensional Analysis

### Finding One: The Formalization-Rhetoric Trade-off Reveals the Argument's Double Architecture

The four dimensions consistently show the same pattern: the 2026 article gains formal precision at the cost of rhetorical force. The vocabulary becomes more technical, the methodology more formal, the metaphors less culturally resonant, and the framing more cautious about particular claims while more confident about overall theoretical standing. This is not merely a change in audience but a **revealing of what the argument actually consists of at its load-bearing level**, separate from its rhetorical superstructure.

What this reveals inferentially: the claims that survive the move from book to article (PMCP/PMBM distinction, falling rate of profit extension, four-phase doom-loop model) are the core theoretical commitments. The claims that don't survive (death of the liberal individual, cloud mobilization as specific tactic, Minotaur mythology) were doing rhetorical work that the formal argument cannot perform. A critic who engaged only with the 2026 article would be engaging with a structurally weaker version of the rhetorical argument but a structurally stronger version of the theoretical argument.

### Finding Two: The Switching-Cost Enrichment Deepens the Self-Undermining Logic

The addition of the switching-cost mechanism (endnote 29) to the cloud serfdom analysis is the most important conceptual enrichment from book to article, and it has an underappreciated implication. If cloud serfs build the walls of their own fief through their unpaid labor — if every post, every like, every purchase raises the cost of exiting — then the mechanism of serfdom is structurally *identical* to the mechanism by which cloud mobilization would need to operate: using cloud platforms to organize resistance on cloud platforms.

The inferential commitment this creates: cloud mobilization cannot be organized through Amazon's platform, Google's search, Meta's social media, or Apple's App Store without simultaneously building the cloud capital that makes these platforms more dominant. The resistance and the oppression share the same infrastructure. The 2026 article's retreat from the specific cloud mobilization concept (absorbed into abstract alliance theory) may reflect an implicit recognition of this problem — the specific tactic doesn't survive the logic of the switching-cost mechanism.

### Finding Three: The Commodity Dispute Is Unresolvable Within the Terms Both Parties Accept

The dimensional analysis makes visible that the commodity concept's formalization (vocabulary shift) and the shift to direct Marx citation (methodological shift) both make the PMCP/PMBM distinction more precise but not more defensible against Morozov's challenge. Morozov's objection is not that Google's services are non-commodities but that they *could be described as* commodities produced for indirect exchange. The definitional dispute is not about Marx's definition but about its application.

The inferential commitment embedded in this impasse: the technofeudal hypothesis cannot be definitively established by definitional argument alone. It requires either (a) empirical demonstration that cloud rents are macroeconomically dominant over profits — which the measurement problem makes impossible — or (b) a different kind of argument that shows the behavioral modification mechanism generates *qualitatively* different dynamics than commodity production, regardless of how one classifies the output. Varoufakis's Section 7 model attempts (b), but it assumes the PMCP/PMBM distinction rather than establishing it. The argument is circular at its core: cloud capital is PMBM because it's unproductive; it's unproductive because its outputs are non-commodities; the outputs are non-commodities because they're produced by PMBM. The circle can only be broken by an argument that is not available in either text.

### Finding Four: The Morozov Response Has Already Changed the Argument

Comparing the two texts reveals that Morozov's critique — though only cited explicitly in the 2026 article — has already altered the book's argument structurally. The book's mythological framing (Minotaur, Hesiod, father-son narrative) is precisely what Morozov called "Victorian melodrama." The 2026 article's elimination of this framing is a response to that critique even when Morozov is only cited in a footnote criticizing "unnecessary nastiness." The argument has been reshaped by the critique even as it denies the critique's fundamental force.

This is characteristic of a paradigm under pressure: the response to criticism is to formalize, not to retract. The formalization makes the argument less vulnerable to the "melodrama" charge while making it more vulnerable to the "measurement impossibility" charge. The argument has traded one vulnerability for another. Whether this is an improvement depends on which vulnerability is more damaging to the argument's overall project.

## VIII. Synthesis: What the Evolution Reveals About the Argument's Architecture

The Koselleckian reading of this conceptual evolution reveals a **double temporality** operating in both texts. The book's concepts primarily inhabit *Erfahrungsraum* (space of experience): they diagnose a transformation that the reader can recognize from lived experience (amazon.com as fief, Alexa as command capital, the 2020 London Stock Exchange spike as evidence). The 2026 article's concepts primarily inhabit *Erwartungshorizont* (horizon of expectation): they generate predictions (doom-loop dynamics, DeepSeek as confirmation, stablecoin as next development) that can only be confirmed by future events.

This temporal shift has political implications that neither text fully acknowledges. Arguments from experience call for *recognition and action now*. Arguments from prediction call for *preparation for what is coming*. The book's political register (cloud rebellion, "we have nothing to lose but our mind-chains!") is calibrated for the first; it is the language of people who recognize their situation and must act. The 2026 article's political register (revolutionary alliances, socialist transformation of cloud capital) is calibrated for the second; it is the language of people preparing for a historical moment that the formal analysis predicts but cannot time.

The most consequential finding of this dimensional analysis is that these two temporal registers are not compatible. The urgency of the book requires the transformation to be *present and recognized*. The formal model of the article requires the transformation to be *in progress and self-undermining*. A system in the process of self-undermining (Phases 3 and 4 of the doom-loop) calls for different political strategy than a system at its moment of maximum dominance. Varoufakis never acknowledges this incompatibility — the political prescription remains constant across both texts even as the theoretical temporality shifts. This is the deepest inferential commitment the evolution reveals: the political conclusion is prior to and independent of the theoretical analysis, attached to it from outside rather than derived from it.
